# Islamic Architecture

## Overview

Islamic architecture encompasses the architectural styles of buildings associated with Islam, from the 7th century to the present. It spans a vast geographic area from Spain to Indonesia and is characterized by geometric patterns, calligraphy, courtyards, domes, and minarets.

## Key Features

- Geometric patterns: complex interlocking patterns based on mathematical principles, reflecting the infinite nature of God
- Calligraphy: Quranic verses and religious texts as decorative elements
- Arabesques: flowing vegetal patterns symbolizing the infinite nature of creation
- Courtyards: central open spaces providing light, air, and privacy
- Muqarnas: honeycomb-like vaulting used in domes, arches, and niches
- Minarets: towers from which the call to prayer is issued
- Mihrab: a niche in the wall indicating the direction of Mecca
- Iwans: large arched portals opening onto courtyards

## Famous Examples

- Dome of the Rock, Jerusalem (691): one of the oldest Islamic buildings, octagonal plan with golden dome
- Great Mosque of Córdoba (784-987): 856 columns creating a forest-like interior, horseshoe arches
- Alhambra, Granada (1238-1358): palace complex with intricate stucco work and water gardens
- Taj Mahal, Agra (1632-1653): white marble mausoleum, considered the finest Mughal architecture
- Sultan Ahmed Mosque (Blue Mosque), Istanbul (1609-1616): six minarets, 20,000 blue Iznik tiles
- Sheikh Zayed Grand Mosque, Abu Dhabi (2007): 82 domes, 1,000 columns, capacity for 40,000 worshippers

## Regional Variations

- Moorish (Spain/North Africa): horseshoe arches, colorful tile work (zellij)
- Persian: pointed arches, colorful tile mosaics, large iwans
- Mughal (India): white marble, pietra dura inlay, symmetrical gardens
- Ottoman (Turkey): large central domes inspired by Hagia Sophia, pencil minarets
- Southeast Asian: tiered roofs, tropical adaptations, wood construction
