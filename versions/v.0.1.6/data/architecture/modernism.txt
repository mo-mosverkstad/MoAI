# Modernist Architecture

## Overview

Modernist architecture emerged in the early 20th century as a rejection of historical ornamentation. Its motto was "form follows function" — buildings should be designed primarily for their purpose, using modern materials like steel, glass, and reinforced concrete.

## Principles

- Rejection of ornament: Adolf Loos declared "ornament is crime" in 1908
- Open floor plans: flexible interior spaces without load-bearing walls
- Honest materials: exposing structural elements rather than hiding them
- Integration with nature: large windows connecting interior and exterior
- Mass production: standardized components for efficient construction
- International Style: universal design principles applicable anywhere

## Key Architects

- Le Corbusier (1887-1965): Swiss-French architect who defined the Five Points of Architecture (pilotis, free plan, free facade, ribbon windows, roof garden). Designed Villa Savoye and Unité d'Habitation.
- Ludwig Mies van der Rohe (1886-1969): German-American architect known for "less is more." Designed the Barcelona Pavilion and Farnsworth House.
- Frank Lloyd Wright (1867-1959): American architect who developed organic architecture. Designed Fallingwater and the Guggenheim Museum.
- Walter Gropius (1883-1969): founded the Bauhaus school in 1919, integrating art, craft, and technology.

## The Bauhaus

The Bauhaus school (1919-1933) in Germany was the most influential art and design school of the 20th century. It sought to unify art, craft, and technology. The Bauhaus building in Dessau (1926) exemplifies its principles: glass curtain walls, flat roof, asymmetric composition. The Nazis closed the school in 1933, and its members emigrated, spreading modernist ideas worldwide.

## Criticism

- Lack of human scale: massive concrete buildings can feel alienating
- Urban renewal failures: modernist housing projects often became sites of poverty and crime
- Cultural insensitivity: the International Style ignored local traditions and climate
- Monotony: repetitive forms across different contexts
