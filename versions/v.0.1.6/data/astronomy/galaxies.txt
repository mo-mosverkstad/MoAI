# Galaxies

## Definition

A galaxy is a gravitationally bound system of stars, stellar remnants, interstellar gas, dust, and dark matter. Galaxies range from dwarfs with a few billion stars to giants with one hundred trillion stars, all orbiting a common center of mass.

## Types of Galaxies

Edwin Hubble classified galaxies by shape (Hubble sequence):

- Spiral galaxies: flat rotating disks with spiral arms, central bulge, and halo. Contain young and old stars, gas, and dust. Examples: Milky Way, Andromeda (M31).
- Elliptical galaxies: smooth, featureless ellipsoids ranging from nearly spherical to elongated. Contain mostly old stars with little gas or dust. Range from dwarf to giant (M87 contains trillions of stars).
- Lenticular galaxies: intermediate between spiral and elliptical — disk-shaped but without prominent spiral arms.
- Irregular galaxies: no distinct shape, often result of gravitational interactions. Examples: Large and Small Magellanic Clouds.

## The Milky Way

Our home galaxy:

- Type: barred spiral galaxy
- Diameter: approximately 100,000 light-years
- Stars: 100-400 billion
- Age: approximately 13.6 billion years
- Central supermassive black hole: Sagittarius A* (4 million solar masses)
- Our Sun is located about 26,000 light-years from the center in the Orion Arm
- Orbital period of the Sun around the galactic center: approximately 225 million years

## Galaxy Formation and Evolution

- Galaxies formed from density fluctuations in the early universe after the Big Bang
- Small protogalaxies merged to form larger galaxies (hierarchical formation)
- Galaxy mergers continue today — the Milky Way will merge with Andromeda in approximately 4.5 billion years
- Active galactic nuclei (AGN): galaxies with extremely luminous centers powered by accreting supermassive black holes
- Quasars: the most luminous AGN, visible across billions of light-years

## Galaxy Clusters

Galaxies are not uniformly distributed but form structures:

- Galaxy groups: 3-30 galaxies (Local Group contains ~80 galaxies including Milky Way and Andromeda)
- Galaxy clusters: hundreds to thousands of galaxies (Virgo Cluster, Coma Cluster)
- Superclusters: clusters of clusters (Laniakea Supercluster contains ~100,000 galaxies)
- Cosmic web: galaxies are distributed along filaments surrounding vast empty voids
