# The Human Body

## Organ Systems

The human body contains 11 major organ systems that work together to maintain life:

- Circulatory system: heart, blood vessels, and blood transport oxygen and nutrients
- Respiratory system: lungs and airways exchange oxygen and carbon dioxide
- Nervous system: brain, spinal cord, and nerves process information and coordinate responses
- Digestive system: breaks down food into nutrients for absorption
- Musculoskeletal system: 206 bones and over 600 muscles provide structure and movement
- Immune system: defends against pathogens using white blood cells and antibodies
- Endocrine system: glands produce hormones that regulate metabolism, growth, and reproduction
- Urinary system: kidneys filter blood and produce urine to remove waste
- Integumentary system: skin, hair, and nails protect against the external environment
- Reproductive system: produces gametes and enables reproduction
- Lymphatic system: drains excess fluid and supports immune function

## The Heart

The human heart is a muscular organ about the size of a fist that pumps blood throughout the body. It beats approximately 100,000 times per day, pumping about 7,500 liters of blood daily.

The heart has four chambers:

- Right atrium: receives deoxygenated blood from the body
- Right ventricle: pumps blood to the lungs for oxygenation
- Left atrium: receives oxygenated blood from the lungs
- Left ventricle: pumps oxygenated blood to the entire body

## The Brain

The human brain weighs approximately 1.4 kg and contains about 86 billion neurons. It consumes 20% of the body's energy despite being only 2% of body weight.

Major brain regions:

- Cerebral cortex: higher thinking, language, consciousness
- Cerebellum: coordination, balance, motor learning
- Brainstem: basic life functions (breathing, heart rate, sleep)
- Hippocampus: memory formation
- Amygdala: emotional processing, fear responses
- Prefrontal cortex: decision-making, planning, personality

## The Immune System

The immune system protects against disease through multiple layers:

- Physical barriers: skin, mucous membranes, stomach acid
- Innate immunity: rapid, non-specific response (inflammation, fever, natural killer cells)
- Adaptive immunity: specific, slower response that creates memory (T cells, B cells, antibodies)

Vaccines work by training the adaptive immune system to recognize pathogens without causing disease, creating immunological memory for future protection.
