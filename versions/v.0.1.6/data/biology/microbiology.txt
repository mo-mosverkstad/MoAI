# Microbiology

## Definition

Microbiology is the study of microscopic organisms — bacteria, viruses, fungi, protozoa, and algae. These organisms are invisible to the naked eye but play essential roles in ecosystems, human health, industry, and agriculture.

## Bacteria

Single-celled prokaryotes, among the oldest life forms on Earth (3.5 billion years):

- Shapes: cocci (spherical), bacilli (rod-shaped), spirilla (spiral)
- Size: typically 1-10 micrometers
- Reproduction: binary fission (can divide every 20 minutes under ideal conditions)
- Metabolism: incredibly diverse — some use sunlight, some use chemicals, some require oxygen, some are poisoned by it
- Beneficial roles: nitrogen fixation, decomposition, gut microbiome, food production (yogurt, cheese)
- Pathogenic bacteria: tuberculosis, cholera, plague, strep throat, food poisoning

## Viruses

Non-living particles that replicate only inside host cells:

- Structure: genetic material (DNA or RNA) enclosed in a protein coat (capsid), sometimes with a lipid envelope
- Size: 20-300 nanometers (much smaller than bacteria)
- Cannot reproduce independently — hijack host cell machinery
- Not considered alive (no metabolism, no cells, cannot reproduce alone)
- Examples: influenza, HIV, SARS-CoV-2, Ebola, rabies, measles
- Bacteriophages: viruses that infect bacteria (most abundant biological entities on Earth)

## Fungi

Eukaryotic organisms that absorb nutrients from their environment:

- Include yeasts (single-celled), molds, and mushrooms (multicellular)
- Cell walls made of chitin (not cellulose like plants)
- Decomposers: break down dead organic matter, recycling nutrients
- Symbiotic: mycorrhizal fungi help 90% of plants absorb water and minerals
- Useful: bread and beer (yeast fermentation), antibiotics (penicillin from Penicillium), food (mushrooms, truffles)
- Pathogenic: athlete's foot, ringworm, candidiasis, aspergillosis

## The Human Microbiome

The human body hosts approximately 38 trillion microorganisms (slightly more than human cells):

- Gut microbiome: 1,000+ bacterial species, weighing 1-2 kg total
- Functions: digestion of fiber, vitamin synthesis (K, B12), immune system training, pathogen resistance
- Disruption (dysbiosis): linked to obesity, inflammatory bowel disease, allergies, depression
- Each person's microbiome is unique (like a fingerprint)
- Established at birth and shaped by diet, environment, and antibiotics

## Applications

- Antibiotics: produced by soil bacteria and fungi (penicillin, streptomycin, tetracycline)
- Biotechnology: bacteria engineered to produce insulin, growth hormone, biofuels
- Food industry: fermentation (beer, wine, cheese, yogurt, soy sauce, kimchi)
- Bioremediation: microbes that break down oil spills and toxic waste
- Agriculture: nitrogen-fixing bacteria reduce need for synthetic fertilizers
