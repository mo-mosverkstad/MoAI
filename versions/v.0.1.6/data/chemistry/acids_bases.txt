# Acids and Bases

## Definition

Acids and bases are fundamental chemical substances that react with each other in neutralization reactions. Several definitions exist:

- Arrhenius (1884): acids produce H⁺ ions in water; bases produce OH⁻ ions
- Brønsted-Lowry (1923): acids donate protons (H⁺); bases accept protons
- Lewis (1923): acids accept electron pairs; bases donate electron pairs

## The pH Scale

pH measures the concentration of hydrogen ions in a solution:

- pH = -log[H⁺]
- Scale runs from 0 to 14
- pH 7: neutral (pure water)
- pH < 7: acidic (more H⁺ ions)
- pH > 7: basic/alkaline (fewer H⁺ ions)
- Each unit represents a 10-fold change in H⁺ concentration

Common pH values:

- Battery acid: pH 0
- Lemon juice: pH 2
- Vinegar: pH 3
- Coffee: pH 5
- Pure water: pH 7
- Baking soda: pH 9
- Ammonia: pH 11
- Bleach: pH 13

## Strong vs. Weak

- Strong acids: completely dissociate in water (HCl, H₂SO₄, HNO₃)
- Weak acids: partially dissociate (acetic acid, carbonic acid, citric acid)
- Strong bases: completely dissociate (NaOH, KOH)
- Weak bases: partially dissociate (ammonia NH₃, baking soda NaHCO₃)

## Neutralization

When an acid reacts with a base, they produce water and a salt:

HCl + NaOH → NaCl + H₂O (hydrochloric acid + sodium hydroxide → table salt + water)

## Buffer Solutions

Buffers resist changes in pH when small amounts of acid or base are added. They consist of a weak acid and its conjugate base (or vice versa). Blood is buffered at pH 7.35-7.45 by the carbonic acid/bicarbonate system — even small deviations can be life-threatening.

## Applications

- Stomach acid (HCl, pH 1.5-3.5): digests food and kills bacteria
- Antacids: neutralize excess stomach acid (calcium carbonate, magnesium hydroxide)
- Batteries: sulfuric acid in lead-acid batteries
- Cleaning products: ammonia and sodium hydroxide dissolve grease
- Food preservation: acetic acid (vinegar), citric acid
- Industrial processes: sulfuric acid is the most produced chemical worldwide
