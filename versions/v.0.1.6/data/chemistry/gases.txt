# Gases and Gas Laws

## Properties of Gases

Gases have distinctive properties compared to solids and liquids:

- No fixed shape or volume: expand to fill their container
- Low density: molecules are far apart
- Highly compressible: volume decreases significantly under pressure
- Diffusion: gases mix completely and spontaneously
- Pressure: gas molecules collide with container walls, exerting force per unit area

## The Gas Laws

Empirical relationships between pressure (P), volume (V), temperature (T), and amount (n):

- Boyle's Law (1662): at constant temperature, pressure and volume are inversely proportional (PV = constant). Doubling pressure halves volume.
- Charles's Law (1787): at constant pressure, volume is directly proportional to absolute temperature (V/T = constant). Heating a gas expands it.
- Avogadro's Law (1811): at constant temperature and pressure, volume is proportional to the number of moles (V/n = constant). Equal volumes of gases contain equal numbers of molecules.
- Gay-Lussac's Law: at constant volume, pressure is directly proportional to temperature.

## Ideal Gas Law

Combining all gas laws: PV = nRT

Where R = 8.314 J/(mol·K) is the universal gas constant.

The ideal gas law assumes:

- Gas molecules have negligible volume
- No intermolecular forces between molecules
- Collisions are perfectly elastic

Real gases deviate from ideal behavior at high pressures and low temperatures (van der Waals equation accounts for this).

## Atmospheric Gases

Earth's atmosphere composition (dry air):

- Nitrogen (N₂): 78.09%
- Oxygen (O₂): 20.95%
- Argon (Ar): 0.93%
- Carbon dioxide (CO₂): 0.042% (420 ppm, rising due to fossil fuel burning)
- Trace gases: neon, helium, methane, krypton, hydrogen, ozone

Atmospheric pressure at sea level: 101,325 Pa (1 atm = 760 mmHg).

## Important Gases

- Oxygen (O₂): essential for respiration, produced by photosynthesis
- Carbon dioxide (CO₂): greenhouse gas, used in photosynthesis, carbonated beverages
- Nitrogen (N₂): inert, used in food packaging, cryogenics (liquid nitrogen at -196°C)
- Helium (He): lighter than air, non-flammable, used in balloons, MRI machines, and cryogenics
- Ozone (O₃): absorbs UV radiation in the stratosphere, toxic at ground level
- Methane (CH₄): natural gas fuel, potent greenhouse gas (80× more warming than CO₂ over 20 years)
