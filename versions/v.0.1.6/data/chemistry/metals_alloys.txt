# Metals and Alloys

## Properties of Metals

Metals share characteristic properties due to metallic bonding (delocalized electrons):

- Electrical conductivity: free electrons carry current (silver is the best conductor, followed by copper)
- Thermal conductivity: free electrons transfer heat efficiently
- Malleability: can be hammered into thin sheets without breaking
- Ductility: can be drawn into wires
- Luster: shiny appearance due to reflection of light by free electrons
- High melting points: strong metallic bonds (tungsten melts at 3,422°C, the highest of any metal)

## Important Metals

- Iron (Fe): most widely used metal, basis of steel, 5% of Earth's crust
- Aluminum (Al): lightweight, corrosion-resistant, most abundant metal in Earth's crust (8%)
- Copper (Cu): excellent conductor, used in wiring and plumbing since antiquity
- Gold (Au): does not corrode, extremely malleable, used in electronics and jewelry
- Silver (Ag): best electrical and thermal conductor, used in electronics and photography
- Titanium (Ti): strong, lightweight, corrosion-resistant, used in aerospace and medical implants
- Platinum (Pt): rare, catalytic properties, used in catalytic converters and jewelry

## Alloys

An alloy is a mixture of metals (or metal with non-metals) designed to have improved properties:

- Steel: iron + carbon (0.2-2.1%). Stronger than pure iron. Stainless steel adds chromium for corrosion resistance.
- Bronze: copper + tin. First alloy used by humans (Bronze Age, 3300 BC). Hard and corrosion-resistant.
- Brass: copper + zinc. Golden color, used in musical instruments and fittings.
- Aluminum alloys: aluminum + copper/magnesium/silicon. Lightweight and strong, used in aircraft.
- Nichrome: nickel + chromium. High electrical resistance, used in heating elements.
- Solder: tin + lead (or tin + silver for lead-free). Low melting point, joins electronic components.

## Corrosion

Corrosion is the degradation of metals through chemical reactions with the environment:

- Rusting: iron reacts with oxygen and water to form iron oxide (Fe₂O₃). Costs the global economy over $2.5 trillion annually.
- Prevention methods: painting, galvanizing (zinc coating), stainless steel (chromium forms protective oxide layer), cathodic protection, anodizing

## Recycling

Metals are infinitely recyclable without loss of properties:

- Aluminum recycling uses 95% less energy than producing from ore
- Steel is the most recycled material in the world
- Precious metals are recovered from electronic waste (urban mining)
- Recycling reduces mining, energy use, and environmental damage
