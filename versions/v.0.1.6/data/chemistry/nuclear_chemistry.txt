# Nuclear Chemistry

## Definition

Nuclear chemistry studies the changes in atomic nuclei — reactions involving protons and neutrons rather than electrons. Nuclear reactions release millions of times more energy than chemical reactions because they involve the strong nuclear force.

## Radioactivity

Radioactivity is the spontaneous emission of particles or energy from unstable atomic nuclei. Discovered by Henri Becquerel in 1896 and studied extensively by Marie and Pierre Curie.

Types of radioactive decay:

- Alpha decay (α): nucleus emits a helium-4 nucleus (2 protons + 2 neutrons). Reduces atomic number by 2. Stopped by paper or skin.
- Beta decay (β): neutron converts to proton (or vice versa), emitting an electron (or positron). Changes atomic number by 1. Stopped by aluminum foil.
- Gamma radiation (γ): high-energy electromagnetic radiation emitted after other decay. No change in atomic number. Requires lead or thick concrete to stop.

## Half-Life

The half-life is the time required for half of a radioactive sample to decay:

- Carbon-14: 5,730 years (used for archaeological dating up to ~50,000 years)
- Uranium-238: 4.5 billion years (used for dating rocks and the age of Earth)
- Iodine-131: 8 days (used in thyroid cancer treatment)
- Polonium-210: 138 days (extremely toxic, used to assassinate Alexander Litvinenko in 2006)
- Francium-223: 22 minutes (most unstable naturally occurring element)

## Nuclear Fission

Fission splits a heavy nucleus into lighter nuclei, releasing enormous energy:

- Uranium-235 absorbs a neutron and splits into two smaller nuclei plus 2-3 neutrons
- Chain reaction: released neutrons cause further fissions
- Critical mass: minimum amount of fissile material needed to sustain a chain reaction
- Nuclear power plants: controlled fission heats water to produce steam that drives turbines
- Nuclear weapons: uncontrolled chain reaction releases energy explosively

## Nuclear Fusion

Fusion combines light nuclei into heavier ones, releasing even more energy than fission:

- Powers the Sun: hydrogen nuclei fuse into helium at 15 million°C
- Requires extreme temperature and pressure to overcome electrostatic repulsion
- Produces no long-lived radioactive waste and uses abundant fuel (hydrogen isotopes)
- ITER (France): international project attempting to achieve sustained fusion by the 2030s
- Currently no commercial fusion power plants exist

## Applications

- Nuclear power: provides approximately 10% of world electricity (440 reactors in 32 countries)
- Medical imaging: PET scans use positron-emitting isotopes
- Cancer treatment: radiation therapy targets tumors
- Carbon dating: determines age of organic materials
- Smoke detectors: contain americium-241 (alpha emitter)
- Food irradiation: gamma rays kill bacteria without making food radioactive
