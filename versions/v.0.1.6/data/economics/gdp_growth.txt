# GDP and Economic Growth

## What is GDP

Gross Domestic Product (GDP) is the total monetary value of all finished goods and services produced within a country's borders in a specific time period. It is the most widely used measure of economic output and is used to compare the economic performance of different countries.

## Measuring GDP

GDP can be calculated three ways (all yield the same result):

- Expenditure approach: GDP = C + I + G + (X - M)
  - C = consumer spending
  - I = business investment
  - G = government spending
  - X - M = net exports (exports minus imports)

- Income approach: sum of all incomes earned in production (wages, profits, rents, interest)

- Production approach: sum of value added at each stage of production

## GDP Per Capita

GDP per capita (GDP divided by population) is a better indicator of individual living standards than total GDP. Countries with the highest GDP per capita (2023) include Luxembourg, Singapore, Ireland, and Norway.

## Limitations of GDP

GDP does not measure:

- Income inequality: a country can have high GDP but extreme poverty
- Non-market activities: household work, volunteer work, subsistence farming
- Environmental degradation: pollution and resource depletion are not subtracted
- Quality of life: leisure time, health, education, happiness
- Underground economy: illegal activities and unreported income

## Economic Growth

Economic growth is the increase in the production of goods and services over time, typically measured as the percentage change in real GDP.

Sources of economic growth:

- Physical capital accumulation: investment in machinery, infrastructure, technology
- Human capital: education, training, and health improvements
- Technological progress: innovation and productivity improvements
- Institutional quality: rule of law, property rights, stable governance
- Trade: specialization and access to larger markets

## Business Cycles

Economies experience recurring fluctuations:

- Expansion: rising GDP, falling unemployment, increasing investment
- Peak: maximum economic output before a downturn
- Recession: two consecutive quarters of declining GDP
- Trough: the lowest point before recovery begins

Central banks use monetary policy (interest rates, money supply) and governments use fiscal policy (taxation, spending) to moderate business cycles.
