# Globalization

## Definition

Globalization is the increasing interconnection of the world's economies, cultures, and populations through cross-border trade, investment, technology, and migration. It has accelerated dramatically since the 1980s due to advances in transportation, communication, and trade liberalization.

## Dimensions of Globalization

- Economic globalization: international trade, foreign direct investment, global supply chains, financial integration
- Cultural globalization: spread of ideas, values, media, food, and fashion across borders
- Political globalization: international organizations, treaties, and governance structures
- Technological globalization: spread of technology, internet connectivity, and digital platforms

## History

- Silk Road (200 BC - 1450 AD): ancient trade routes connecting China, Central Asia, and Europe
- Age of Exploration (1500s): European maritime trade creates first global connections
- First wave of globalization (1870-1914): steamships, telegraph, gold standard enable massive trade growth
- Interwar deglobalization (1914-1945): World Wars and protectionism reverse integration
- Bretton Woods era (1945-1971): IMF, World Bank, GATT rebuild international economic order
- Hyperglobalization (1990-2008): fall of communism, WTO, internet, China's rise
- Slowbalization (2008-present): financial crisis, trade wars, pandemic disrupt global integration

## Benefits

- Economic growth: trade has contributed to unprecedented global prosperity
- Poverty reduction: integration into global markets lifted billions from poverty
- Consumer choice: access to diverse products at lower prices
- Technology transfer: developing countries gain access to advanced technologies
- Cultural exchange: exposure to diverse perspectives and ideas

## Criticisms

- Job displacement: manufacturing jobs moved from developed to developing countries
- Rising inequality: benefits concentrated among skilled workers and capital owners
- Environmental damage: increased production and transportation raise emissions
- Cultural homogenization: local traditions displaced by global (often Western) culture
- Race to the bottom: countries compete by lowering labor and environmental standards
- Financial contagion: crises spread rapidly across interconnected economies

## Global Supply Chains

Modern products are assembled from components made in multiple countries:

- An iPhone contains parts from over 40 countries
- A typical car has 30,000 parts from hundreds of suppliers worldwide
- COVID-19 exposed vulnerabilities: semiconductor shortages, shipping disruptions
- Reshoring and nearshoring: trend toward bringing production closer to home markets
- Just-in-time vs. just-in-case: shifting from efficiency to resilience
