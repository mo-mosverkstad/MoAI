# Poverty and Inequality

## Defining Poverty

Poverty is the state of having insufficient resources to meet basic human needs (food, shelter, clothing, healthcare, education).

- Absolute poverty: living below a minimum income threshold. The World Bank defines extreme poverty as living on less than $2.15 per day (2022 purchasing power parity).
- Relative poverty: having significantly less income than the median in one's society (typically below 50-60% of median income).
- Multidimensional poverty: measured by deprivations in health, education, and living standards (not just income).

## Global Poverty Statistics

- Approximately 700 million people live in extreme poverty (2024)
- Down from 1.9 billion in 1990 — the greatest reduction in poverty in human history
- Sub-Saharan Africa has the highest poverty rates (approximately 35%)
- East Asia achieved the most dramatic reduction (China lifted 800 million from poverty since 1980)
- COVID-19 pandemic pushed approximately 70 million people back into extreme poverty

## Income Inequality

Inequality measures how unevenly income or wealth is distributed:

- Gini coefficient: ranges from 0 (perfect equality) to 1 (one person has everything). Most countries range from 0.25 (Scandinavia) to 0.65 (South Africa).
- Top 1% share: the richest 1% own approximately 46% of global wealth (2023)
- Bottom 50% share: the poorest half of humanity owns approximately 2% of global wealth
- Within-country inequality has risen in most developed nations since the 1980s
- Between-country inequality has decreased as developing nations grow faster

## Causes of Poverty

- Lack of education and skills
- Unemployment and underemployment
- Discrimination (gender, race, caste, disability)
- Conflict and political instability
- Geographic isolation and lack of infrastructure
- Health problems and disability
- Intergenerational transmission (poverty trap)

## Poverty Reduction Strategies

- Economic growth: historically the most powerful poverty reducer
- Education: each additional year of schooling increases earnings by approximately 10%
- Social safety nets: cash transfers, food assistance, unemployment insurance
- Healthcare access: preventing catastrophic medical expenses
- Microfinance: small loans enabling entrepreneurship
- Infrastructure: roads, electricity, clean water, internet access
- Land reform and property rights: enabling asset accumulation
