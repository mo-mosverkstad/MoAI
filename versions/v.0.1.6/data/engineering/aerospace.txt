# Aerospace Engineering

## Definition

Aerospace engineering is the field of engineering concerned with the development of aircraft and spacecraft. It is divided into aeronautical engineering (atmospheric flight) and astronautical engineering (space flight).

## Principles of Flight

Four forces act on an aircraft in flight:

- Lift: upward force generated by wings (Bernoulli's principle and Newton's third law). Air moves faster over the curved upper surface, creating lower pressure above the wing.
- Weight (gravity): downward force due to the aircraft's mass
- Thrust: forward force from engines (propellers, jets, rockets)
- Drag: backward force from air resistance (friction and pressure drag)

For level flight: lift = weight, thrust = drag.

## Aircraft Types

- Fixed-wing aircraft: airplanes with rigid wings (commercial airliners, fighters, gliders)
- Rotary-wing aircraft: helicopters with spinning rotor blades providing lift
- Tiltrotor: combines helicopter and airplane capabilities (V-22 Osprey)
- Unmanned aerial vehicles (UAVs/drones): remotely piloted or autonomous
- Supersonic aircraft: fly faster than the speed of sound (Mach 1 = 1,235 km/h at sea level). Concorde flew at Mach 2.04.
- Hypersonic vehicles: above Mach 5 (experimental, military applications)

## Jet Engines

- Turbojet: compresses air, adds fuel, ignites, expels hot gas (early jets)
- Turbofan: large fan bypasses most air around the core (modern airliners — quieter, more efficient)
- Turboprop: jet engine drives a propeller (regional aircraft, cargo planes)
- Ramjet/scramjet: no moving parts, uses forward motion to compress air (hypersonic vehicles)
- Rocket engines: carry both fuel and oxidizer, work in vacuum (space launch)

## Spacecraft

- Launch vehicles: rockets that deliver payloads to orbit (Saturn V, Falcon 9, Ariane 5, SLS)
- Satellites: orbit Earth for communication, navigation, weather, observation, science
- Space stations: habitable structures in orbit (ISS: 420,000 kg, 109 m wide)
- Interplanetary probes: explore other planets and moons (Voyager, Cassini, Perseverance)
- Reusable rockets: SpaceX Falcon 9 lands and flies again (reduced launch costs by ~90%)

## Materials

- Aluminum alloys: lightweight, corrosion-resistant (traditional aircraft material)
- Carbon fiber composites: stronger and lighter than aluminum (Boeing 787 is 50% composite)
- Titanium: high strength-to-weight ratio, heat resistant (jet engine components)
- Nickel superalloys: withstand extreme temperatures in turbine blades (1,700°C)
- Thermal protection systems: ceramic tiles and ablative materials for reentry (Space Shuttle, Starship)

## Challenges

- Weight reduction: every kilogram saved reduces fuel consumption
- Fuel efficiency: aviation accounts for 2.5% of global CO₂ emissions
- Sustainable aviation fuel (SAF): biofuels and synthetic fuels to reduce emissions
- Electric and hydrogen aircraft: under development for short-range flights
- Space debris: over 36,000 tracked objects in orbit threaten operational satellites
