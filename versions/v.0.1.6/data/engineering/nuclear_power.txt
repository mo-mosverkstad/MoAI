# Nuclear Power

## How It Works

Nuclear power plants generate electricity through controlled nuclear fission. Uranium-235 atoms are split by neutrons, releasing enormous energy as heat. This heat boils water into steam, which drives turbines connected to generators.

## Reactor Types

- Pressurized Water Reactor (PWR): most common type (about 70% of reactors worldwide). Water under high pressure transfers heat from the core to a secondary steam loop.
- Boiling Water Reactor (BWR): water boils directly in the reactor core, producing steam that drives turbines.
- CANDU (Canada): uses heavy water as moderator, can use natural (unenriched) uranium fuel.
- Fast breeder reactors: use fast neutrons, can produce more fissile material than they consume.
- Small Modular Reactors (SMRs): factory-built, smaller capacity (under 300 MW), potentially safer and cheaper.

## Global Status

- 440 operational reactors in 32 countries (2024)
- Provides approximately 10% of world electricity
- France: 70% nuclear electricity (highest proportion)
- United States: 93 reactors, largest nuclear fleet
- China: rapidly expanding (55 reactors, 22 under construction)
- Countries phasing out: Germany (closed last reactor 2023), Italy, Belgium

## Advantages

- Low carbon emissions: lifecycle emissions comparable to wind power (12 g CO₂/kWh)
- High energy density: 1 kg of uranium produces as much energy as 14,000 kg of coal
- Reliable baseload power: operates 24/7 regardless of weather (capacity factor ~90%)
- Small land footprint: compared to solar and wind for equivalent output
- Long operational life: 40-80 years per reactor

## Disadvantages and Risks

- Radioactive waste: spent fuel remains dangerous for thousands of years. No permanent repository yet operational (Finland's Onkalo facility expected to open ~2025).
- Accident risk: Three Mile Island (1979), Chernobyl (1986), Fukushima (2011)
- High construction costs: often over budget and behind schedule ($10-30 billion per reactor)
- Proliferation: nuclear technology can be diverted to weapons programs
- Public opposition: fear of radiation and accidents limits political support
- Long construction time: typically 10-15 years from decision to operation

## Nuclear Safety

Modern reactors incorporate multiple safety systems:

- Defense in depth: multiple independent barriers (fuel cladding, pressure vessel, containment building)
- Passive safety: systems that function without power or human intervention (gravity-fed cooling)
- Negative temperature coefficient: reactor power decreases automatically if temperature rises
- Post-Fukushima improvements: enhanced flood protection, backup power, hydrogen management
