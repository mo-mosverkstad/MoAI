# Renewable Energy

## Overview

Renewable energy comes from sources that are naturally replenished on a human timescale. In 2022, renewables accounted for approximately 30% of global electricity generation. The transition from fossil fuels to renewables is essential for mitigating climate change.

## Solar Energy

Solar photovoltaic (PV) cells convert sunlight directly into electricity using the photovoltaic effect. Solar is the fastest-growing energy source globally.

- Efficiency: commercial panels achieve 20-23% efficiency; laboratory cells exceed 47%
- Cost: has fallen 89% since 2010, now the cheapest source of electricity in most regions
- Capacity: global installed capacity exceeded 1,000 GW in 2022
- Limitations: intermittent (no generation at night), requires large areas, manufacturing uses energy

## Wind Energy

Wind turbines convert kinetic energy of wind into electricity through rotating blades connected to a generator.

- Onshore wind: turbines on land, typically 2-5 MW capacity each
- Offshore wind: turbines in ocean waters, larger (up to 15 MW), stronger and more consistent winds
- Global capacity: approximately 900 GW installed by 2022
- Limitations: intermittent, visual impact, noise, bird and bat mortality

## Hydroelectric Power

Hydropower generates electricity from flowing water, typically using dams to create reservoirs.

- Largest source of renewable electricity globally (about 16% of world electricity)
- Three Gorges Dam (China): world's largest power station at 22.5 GW capacity
- Pumped-storage: uses excess electricity to pump water uphill, then releases it to generate power during peak demand
- Limitations: environmental impact on rivers and ecosystems, displacement of communities, drought vulnerability

## Other Renewables

- Geothermal: heat from Earth's interior (Iceland generates 25% of electricity from geothermal)
- Biomass: organic material burned or converted to fuel (wood, crop waste, biogas)
- Tidal and wave: energy from ocean movements (still largely experimental)
- Hydrogen: produced by electrolysis using renewable electricity (green hydrogen)

## Energy Storage

Storage is critical for managing intermittent renewable sources:

- Lithium-ion batteries: dominant technology for grid storage and electric vehicles
- Pumped hydro: accounts for 95% of global energy storage capacity
- Compressed air: stores energy by compressing air in underground caverns
- Flow batteries: use liquid electrolytes for long-duration storage
- Green hydrogen: excess renewable electricity produces hydrogen for later use
