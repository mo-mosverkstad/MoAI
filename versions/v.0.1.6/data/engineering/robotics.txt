# Robotics

## Definition

Robotics is the interdisciplinary field of engineering and science that deals with the design, construction, operation, and use of robots. A robot is a programmable machine capable of carrying out complex actions automatically, often interacting with the physical world through sensors and actuators.

## History

- 1920: Karel Čapek coins the word "robot" in his play R.U.R. (from Czech robota, meaning forced labor)
- 1954: George Devol patents the first programmable robot (Unimate)
- 1961: Unimate installed at General Motors — first industrial robot
- 1969: Stanford Arm — first electrically powered computer-controlled robot arm
- 1997: Sojourner rover explores Mars
- 2000: Honda's ASIMO humanoid robot walks, climbs stairs, recognizes faces
- 2004: Boston Dynamics' BigDog — quadruped robot for rough terrain
- 2011: IBM Watson wins Jeopardy! against human champions
- 2020: Spot (Boston Dynamics) commercially available quadruped robot

## Types of Robots

- Industrial robots: perform manufacturing tasks (welding, painting, assembly, pick-and-place). Over 3.5 million operational worldwide.
- Service robots: assist humans in non-manufacturing settings (vacuum cleaners, lawn mowers, delivery robots)
- Medical robots: surgical systems (da Vinci), rehabilitation, prosthetics, pharmacy automation
- Mobile robots: navigate environments autonomously (autonomous vehicles, drones, Mars rovers)
- Humanoid robots: human-shaped for interaction (ASIMO, Atlas, Sophia)
- Collaborative robots (cobots): designed to work safely alongside humans without cages
- Swarm robots: large numbers of simple robots coordinating collectively

## Key Components

- Sensors: cameras, LIDAR, ultrasonic, force/torque, IMU (inertial measurement unit)
- Actuators: electric motors, hydraulic cylinders, pneumatic systems, artificial muscles
- Controllers: computers that process sensor data and command actuators
- End effectors: grippers, tools, or specialized attachments for specific tasks
- Power supply: batteries, tethered power, solar panels

## Challenges

- Perception: understanding unstructured environments (far harder than controlled factories)
- Manipulation: grasping diverse objects with varying shapes, sizes, and materials
- Locomotion: walking, running, climbing on uneven terrain
- Human-robot interaction: natural communication, safety, trust
- Autonomy: making decisions without human intervention in novel situations
- Energy: batteries limit operational time for mobile robots

## Future Directions

- Soft robotics: flexible materials inspired by biological organisms
- AI integration: deep learning for perception, planning, and manipulation
- Human augmentation: exoskeletons for strength, rehabilitation, and mobility
- Autonomous vehicles: self-driving cars, trucks, and delivery drones
- Space robotics: construction and maintenance in orbit, planetary exploration
