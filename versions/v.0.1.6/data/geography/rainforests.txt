# Rainforests

## Definition

Tropical rainforests are dense, warm, wet forests found near the equator. They receive at least 1,750 mm of rainfall per year (some exceed 10,000 mm) and maintain temperatures between 20-34°C year-round with minimal seasonal variation.

## Distribution

Tropical rainforests are found in three main regions:

- Central and South America: Amazon basin (largest), Central American forests
- Central and West Africa: Congo basin (second largest), West African forests
- Southeast Asia and Oceania: Indonesia, Malaysia, Papua New Guinea, northeastern Australia

Rainforests cover approximately 6% of Earth's land surface (down from 14% historically).

## Structure

Rainforests have distinct vertical layers:

- Emergent layer (45-55 m): tallest trees that rise above the canopy, exposed to wind and sun
- Canopy (25-45 m): continuous layer of treetops forming a "roof." Receives most sunlight. Most biodiversity lives here.
- Understory (5-25 m): shade-tolerant trees and shrubs, receives only 2-5% of sunlight
- Forest floor (0-5 m): very dark, decomposing leaves, fungi, insects. Surprisingly thin soil.

## Biodiversity

Rainforests contain over 50% of all species on Earth despite covering only 6% of land:

- Amazon alone contains approximately 10% of all species on Earth
- One hectare of rainforest may contain 300+ tree species (temperate forests: 10-20)
- Insects: millions of species, many undescribed (one tree in Panama yielded 1,200 beetle species)
- Plants: 80,000 plant species in the Amazon alone
- Animals: jaguars, gorillas, orangutans, toucans, poison dart frogs, anacondas
- New species discovered regularly (average 2 new species per week in the Amazon)

## Ecosystem Services

- Carbon storage: tropical forests store approximately 250 billion tonnes of carbon
- Oxygen production: often called "lungs of the Earth" (though most oxygen is consumed locally)
- Water cycle: trees release water vapor through transpiration, generating rainfall (the Amazon creates 50% of its own rain)
- Medicine: 25% of modern medicines derive from rainforest plants (quinine, curare, vincristine)
- Climate regulation: cooling effect through evapotranspiration

## Deforestation

Tropical deforestation is one of the most urgent environmental crises:

- Rate: approximately 10 million hectares lost per year (2015-2020)
- Amazon: lost approximately 17% of its forest in the last 50 years
- Causes: cattle ranching (80% in Amazon), soy and palm oil plantations, logging, mining, road building
- Consequences: biodiversity loss, carbon emissions (10% of global total), disrupted water cycles, indigenous displacement
- Tipping point: scientists warn the Amazon may be approaching a point where it converts to savanna

## Conservation

- Protected areas: national parks, indigenous reserves, biological corridors
- REDD+ (Reducing Emissions from Deforestation): financial incentives to keep forests standing
- Sustainable forestry: selective logging with replanting
- Consumer pressure: certification schemes (FSC timber, RSPO palm oil)
- Indigenous land rights: indigenous-managed forests have lower deforestation rates
