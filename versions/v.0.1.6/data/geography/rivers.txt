# Rivers

## Definition

A river is a natural flowing watercourse that moves water from higher to lower elevations, eventually reaching an ocean, lake, or another river. Rivers are vital to human civilization — providing water, food, transportation, energy, and fertile land.

## Major Rivers of the World

- Nile (Africa): 6,650 km, longest river (disputed with Amazon). Flows north through 11 countries to the Mediterranean. Sustained ancient Egyptian civilization.
- Amazon (South America): 6,400 km, largest by discharge (209,000 m³/s — 20% of all river water entering the ocean). Drains 7 million km² of rainforest.
- Yangtze (China): 6,300 km, longest in Asia. Three Gorges Dam is the world's largest hydroelectric facility.
- Mississippi-Missouri (North America): 6,275 km system, drains 40% of the continental United States.
- Congo (Africa): 4,700 km, deepest river (220 m). Second-largest by discharge.
- Ganges (India): 2,525 km, sacred in Hinduism. Supports 400 million people in its basin.
- Danube (Europe): 2,850 km, flows through 10 countries — more than any other river.

## River Features

- Source: where a river begins (spring, glacier, lake, or wetland)
- Tributary: a smaller river that flows into a larger one
- Confluence: where two rivers meet
- Meander: a curve or bend in a river (forms on flat terrain)
- Floodplain: flat area adjacent to a river that floods periodically
- Delta: fan-shaped deposit of sediment where a river meets the sea (Nile Delta, Mississippi Delta)
- Estuary: where a river meets the sea, mixing fresh and salt water (tidal influence)
- Watershed (drainage basin): the entire area that drains into a river system

## The Water Cycle and Rivers

Rivers are part of the global water cycle:

1. Evaporation from oceans and lakes
2. Precipitation falls on land
3. Runoff collects in streams and rivers
4. Rivers carry water back to the ocean
5. Groundwater slowly feeds rivers between rain events (baseflow)

## Human Use of Rivers

- Drinking water: rivers supply water to billions of people
- Agriculture: irrigation from rivers feeds much of the world's population
- Hydroelectric power: dams convert river flow to electricity (16% of world electricity)
- Transportation: rivers have been highways of commerce for millennia
- Fishing: freshwater fish provide protein for hundreds of millions
- Recreation: swimming, boating, fishing, tourism

## Threats to Rivers

- Pollution: industrial waste, agricultural runoff, sewage
- Damming: over 57,000 large dams worldwide fragment river ecosystems
- Over-extraction: some rivers (Colorado, Yellow) no longer reach the sea in dry years
- Climate change: altered precipitation patterns, glacier melt, increased flooding and drought
- Invasive species: disrupt native ecosystems (Asian carp in Mississippi, zebra mussels in Great Lakes)
