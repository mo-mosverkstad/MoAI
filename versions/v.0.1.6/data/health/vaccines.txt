# Vaccines

## How Vaccines Work

Vaccines train the immune system to recognize and fight specific pathogens without causing the disease. They introduce a harmless form of the pathogen (or a piece of it) to stimulate an immune response. The immune system then creates memory cells that provide rapid protection if the real pathogen is encountered later.

## Types of Vaccines

- Live attenuated: weakened form of the pathogen (MMR, chickenpox, oral polio)
- Inactivated: killed pathogen (flu shot, hepatitis A, rabies)
- Subunit/conjugate: purified pieces of the pathogen (hepatitis B, HPV, pertussis)
- Toxoid: inactivated toxin produced by the pathogen (tetanus, diphtheria)
- mRNA: genetic instructions for making a viral protein (COVID-19 Pfizer and Moderna vaccines)
- Viral vector: harmless virus delivers genetic material from the target pathogen (COVID-19 AstraZeneca, Ebola)

## History of Vaccination

- 1796: Edward Jenner demonstrates that cowpox infection protects against smallpox
- 1885: Louis Pasteur develops the rabies vaccine
- 1921: BCG vaccine for tuberculosis introduced
- 1955: Jonas Salk's polio vaccine licensed
- 1967: WHO launches the Smallpox Eradication Programme
- 1980: WHO declares smallpox eradicated — the first disease eliminated by vaccination
- 1988: Global Polio Eradication Initiative launched
- 2020: COVID-19 mRNA vaccines developed in under one year

## Herd Immunity

Herd immunity occurs when a sufficient proportion of a population is immune to a disease, making its spread unlikely. The threshold varies by disease:

- Measles: approximately 95% immunity needed (highly contagious, R₀ ≈ 12-18)
- Polio: approximately 80-85% immunity needed
- COVID-19: estimated 70-90% depending on variant

## Vaccine Safety

Vaccines undergo rigorous testing before approval:

- Phase I: small group (20-100), tests safety and dosage
- Phase II: larger group (hundreds), tests efficacy and side effects
- Phase III: thousands of participants, confirms effectiveness and monitors adverse reactions
- Post-market surveillance: ongoing monitoring after approval

Common side effects are mild and temporary: soreness at injection site, low-grade fever, fatigue. Serious adverse events are extremely rare (typically less than 1 in a million doses).

## Impact

Vaccines prevent an estimated 4-5 million deaths per year worldwide. Diseases that were once common killers — smallpox, polio, measles, diphtheria — have been eliminated or dramatically reduced through vaccination programs.
