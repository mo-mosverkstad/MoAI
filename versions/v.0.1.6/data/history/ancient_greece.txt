# Ancient Greece

## Overview

Ancient Greek civilization (c. 800 BC - 146 BC) laid the foundations of Western culture in philosophy, science, mathematics, art, literature, theater, democracy, and athletics. Despite never being a unified nation, the Greek city-states (poleis) shared language, religion, and cultural identity.

## City-States

- Athens: birthplace of democracy, philosophy, and theater. Population ~300,000 at its peak (including slaves and non-citizens).
- Sparta: militaristic society focused on discipline and warfare. Boys entered military training at age 7.
- Corinth: wealthy trading city controlling the isthmus between mainland Greece and the Peloponnese.
- Thebes: briefly dominant after defeating Sparta (371 BC).

## Athenian Democracy

Athens developed the world's first democracy (from demos = people, kratos = power) around 508 BC under Cleisthenes:

- All male citizens over 18 could vote directly on laws and policies (direct democracy)
- Assembly (Ekklesia): met 40 times per year, any citizen could speak
- Council of 500 (Boule): prepared agenda, selected by lottery
- Jury courts: large juries (201-501 citizens) decided legal cases
- Limitations: women, slaves, and foreigners (approximately 80% of population) were excluded

## Philosophy

Greek philosophy shaped Western thought for over 2,000 years:

- Pre-Socratics: sought natural explanations for the world (Thales, Heraclitus, Democritus — who proposed atoms)
- Socrates (470-399 BC): questioned everything through dialogue (Socratic method). Executed for "corrupting the youth."
- Plato (428-348 BC): Theory of Forms, The Republic, founded the Academy
- Aristotle (384-322 BC): logic, biology, ethics, politics, physics. Tutor to Alexander the Great.
- Epicurus: happiness through simple pleasures and freedom from fear
- Stoics (Zeno): virtue and reason, acceptance of what cannot be changed

## Science and Mathematics

- Pythagoras (c. 570-495 BC): Pythagorean theorem, mathematics as the language of nature
- Euclid (c. 300 BC): Elements — systematic geometry, standard textbook for 2,000 years
- Archimedes (287-212 BC): buoyancy, levers, pi calculation, war machines
- Hippocrates (460-370 BC): father of medicine, Hippocratic Oath, natural causes of disease
- Eratosthenes (276-194 BC): calculated Earth's circumference with remarkable accuracy

## Alexander the Great

Alexander III of Macedon (356-323 BC) conquered the largest empire the world had seen by age 30:

- Conquered Persia, Egypt, Central Asia, and reached India
- Founded over 20 cities (including Alexandria in Egypt)
- Spread Greek culture across the known world (Hellenistic period)
- Died at 32, possibly from fever, in Babylon
- His empire fragmented after his death into successor kingdoms

## Legacy

- Democracy: model for modern representative government
- Philosophy: foundation of Western intellectual tradition
- Science: empirical observation and rational inquiry
- Olympics: athletic competition revived in 1896
- Theater: tragedy (Sophocles, Euripides) and comedy (Aristophanes)
- Architecture: columns and proportions still used worldwide
