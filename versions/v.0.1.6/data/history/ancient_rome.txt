# Ancient Rome

## Overview

Ancient Rome was a civilization that grew from a small town on the Tiber River in central Italy into an empire that encompassed most of continental Europe, Britain, western Asia, northern Africa, and the Mediterranean islands. Roman civilization lasted from the founding of Rome in 753 BC to the fall of the Western Roman Empire in 476 AD.

## The Roman Republic (509-27 BC)

The Roman Republic was established in 509 BC after the overthrow of the last Roman king, Lucius Tarquinius Superbus. The Republic was governed by elected magistrates and the Senate.

Key features of the Republic:

- Two consuls served as chief executives, elected annually
- The Senate advised magistrates and controlled finances
- Popular assemblies passed laws and elected officials
- The Tribune of the Plebs could veto actions that harmed common citizens
- Separation of powers prevented any single individual from gaining too much control

## The Roman Empire (27 BC - 476 AD)

Augustus (Octavian) became the first Roman Emperor in 27 BC, ending the Republic. The Pax Romana (27 BC - 180 AD) was a period of relative peace and stability across the empire.

Notable emperors:

- Augustus (27 BC - 14 AD): established the principate, reformed the military
- Trajan (98-117 AD): expanded the empire to its greatest territorial extent
- Hadrian (117-138 AD): consolidated borders, built Hadrian's Wall in Britain
- Marcus Aurelius (161-180 AD): philosopher-emperor, last of the Five Good Emperors
- Constantine (306-337 AD): legalized Christianity, founded Constantinople

## Roman Engineering

Romans were master engineers whose innovations still influence modern infrastructure:

- Aqueducts: transported water over long distances using gravity (the Pont du Gard spans 275 meters)
- Roads: over 400,000 km of roads connected the empire, many still traceable today
- Concrete: Roman concrete (opus caementicium) used volcanic ash and is more durable than modern Portland cement
- Arches and domes: the Pantheon's unreinforced concrete dome (43.3 meters) remained the world's largest for 1,300 years
- Sewage systems: the Cloaca Maxima drained the Roman Forum and is still in use

## Fall of the Western Empire

The Western Roman Empire fell in 476 AD when the Germanic chieftain Odoacer deposed the last emperor, Romulus Augustulus. Contributing factors included:

- Military overextension and reliance on mercenaries
- Economic decline and heavy taxation
- Political instability (over 50 emperors in the 3rd century alone)
- Barbarian invasions by Visigoths, Vandals, and Huns
- Division of the empire into Eastern and Western halves in 285 AD

The Eastern Roman Empire (Byzantine Empire) continued until 1453 when Constantinople fell to the Ottoman Turks.
