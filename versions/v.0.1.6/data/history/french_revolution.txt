# The French Revolution

## Overview

The French Revolution (1789-1799) was a period of radical political and social transformation in France that overthrew the monarchy, established a republic, and culminated in Napoleon's rise to power. It profoundly influenced modern political thought and inspired revolutions worldwide.

## Causes

- Financial crisis: France was nearly bankrupt from wars (including supporting the American Revolution) and extravagant royal spending
- Social inequality: the Three Estates system — clergy (First Estate) and nobility (Second Estate) paid few taxes while the common people (Third Estate, 98% of population) bore the burden
- Enlightenment ideas: philosophers like Voltaire, Rousseau, and Montesquieu challenged absolute monarchy and promoted liberty, equality, and popular sovereignty
- Food shortages: poor harvests in 1788 caused bread prices to soar, leading to widespread hunger
- Weak leadership: King Louis XVI was indecisive and unable to implement reforms

## Key Events

- May 5, 1789: Estates-General convened (first time since 1614)
- June 20, 1789: Tennis Court Oath — Third Estate vows not to disband until a constitution is written
- July 14, 1789: Storming of the Bastille — symbol of royal tyranny, now France's national day
- August 26, 1789: Declaration of the Rights of Man and of the Citizen adopted
- October 1789: Women's March on Versailles forces royal family to Paris
- June 1791: Royal family's failed escape attempt (Flight to Varennes)
- September 1792: Monarchy abolished, French Republic proclaimed
- January 21, 1793: King Louis XVI executed by guillotine
- 1793-1794: Reign of Terror — Robespierre's Committee of Public Safety executes 16,000-40,000 people
- July 1794: Thermidorian Reaction — Robespierre himself guillotined
- November 1799: Napoleon Bonaparte seizes power in a coup d'état

## Legacy

- Abolished feudalism and aristocratic privilege
- Established principles of citizenship, secular government, and legal equality
- Declaration of the Rights of Man influenced the Universal Declaration of Human Rights (1948)
- Inspired revolutions across Europe and Latin America
- Demonstrated both the promise and dangers of revolutionary change
- Napoleonic Code (1804) reformed legal systems across Europe
