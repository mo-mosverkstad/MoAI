# The Renaissance

## Overview

The Renaissance ("rebirth") was a cultural, artistic, and intellectual movement that began in Italy in the 14th century and spread across Europe through the 17th century. It marked the transition from the medieval period to the modern age, characterized by renewed interest in classical Greek and Roman culture, humanism, and scientific inquiry.

## Origins

- Began in Italian city-states (Florence, Venice, Rome) due to their wealth from trade, banking, and patronage
- Rediscovery of classical texts preserved in Islamic libraries and Byzantine manuscripts
- Fall of Constantinople (1453) drove Greek scholars westward, bringing classical knowledge
- Wealthy patrons (Medici family in Florence, popes in Rome) funded artists and scholars
- Black Death (1347-1351) disrupted feudal society, creating social mobility and new thinking

## Humanism

The intellectual foundation of the Renaissance:

- Focus on human potential, achievement, and dignity rather than solely divine matters
- Study of classical languages (Latin, Greek) and literature (studia humanitatis)
- Emphasis on education, rhetoric, history, poetry, and moral philosophy
- Key humanists: Petrarch (father of humanism), Erasmus, Thomas More, Pico della Mirandola
- "Man is the measure of all things" — revival of Protagoras's ancient Greek idea

## Art

Renaissance art revolutionized visual representation:

- Linear perspective: mathematical technique creating illusion of depth (Brunelleschi, 1415)
- Chiaroscuro: dramatic use of light and shadow to create volume
- Anatomical accuracy: artists studied human anatomy (Leonardo dissected cadavers)
- Oil painting: enabled richer colors and finer detail (developed in the Netherlands)

Great artists:

- Leonardo da Vinci (1452-1519): Mona Lisa, The Last Supper, Vitruvian Man. Polymath — artist, scientist, engineer, anatomist.
- Michelangelo (1475-1564): Sistine Chapel ceiling, David, Pietà. Sculptor, painter, architect, poet.
- Raphael (1483-1520): The School of Athens, Sistine Madonna. Harmonious compositions.
- Sandro Botticelli (1445-1510): The Birth of Venus, Primavera.
- Titian (1488-1576): Venetian master of color and portraiture.

## Science

The Renaissance laid groundwork for the Scientific Revolution:

- Nicolaus Copernicus (1473-1543): heliocentric model (Sun at center of solar system)
- Andreas Vesalius (1514-1564): modern anatomy based on dissection (De Humani Corporis Fabrica)
- Galileo Galilei (1564-1642): telescope observations confirmed Copernican model
- Francis Bacon (1561-1626): empirical scientific method

## Legacy

- Established the individual as a creative agent
- Transformed art, architecture, literature, and science
- Printing press (Gutenberg, 1440) spread Renaissance ideas rapidly
- Led directly to the Scientific Revolution and the Enlightenment
- Shaped modern Western concepts of education, citizenship, and human rights
