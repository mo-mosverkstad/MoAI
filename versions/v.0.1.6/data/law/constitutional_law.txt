# Constitutional Law

## Definition

Constitutional law is the body of law that defines the structure of government, the distribution of power among its branches, and the fundamental rights of citizens. A constitution is the supreme law of a nation — all other laws must conform to it.

## Key Principles

- Separation of powers: dividing government into legislative, executive, and judicial branches to prevent concentration of power
- Checks and balances: each branch can limit the others (presidential veto, judicial review, legislative override)
- Federalism: division of power between national and regional governments
- Rule of law: government officials are bound by law, not above it
- Judicial review: courts can declare laws unconstitutional (established in Marbury v. Madison, 1803)

## Fundamental Rights

Most constitutions protect individual rights:

- Freedom of speech and press
- Freedom of religion
- Right to privacy
- Right to due process and fair trial
- Protection against unreasonable search and seizure
- Equal protection under the law
- Right to vote
- Protection against cruel and unusual punishment

## Notable Constitutions

- United States Constitution (1787): the oldest written national constitution still in force. Contains 7 articles and 27 amendments. The Bill of Rights (first 10 amendments) was ratified in 1791.
- French Declaration of the Rights of Man (1789): established principles of popular sovereignty and individual rights during the French Revolution.
- German Basic Law (1949): created after World War II with strong protections for human dignity and democratic principles.
- South African Constitution (1996): widely regarded as one of the most progressive, with extensive socioeconomic rights.

## Constitutional Amendment

Constitutions can be changed through formal amendment processes, which are typically more difficult than passing ordinary legislation to ensure stability. The US Constitution requires two-thirds of Congress and three-fourths of state legislatures to ratify an amendment.
