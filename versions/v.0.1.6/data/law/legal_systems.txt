# Legal Systems

## What is Law

Law is a system of rules created and enforced by social or governmental institutions to regulate behavior. It shapes politics, economics, and society in fundamental ways. Legal systems provide mechanisms for resolving disputes, punishing wrongdoing, and protecting individual rights.

## Major Legal Traditions

- Common law: developed in England, based on judicial precedent (case law). Used in the UK, US, Canada, Australia, and India. Judges interpret and develop law through their decisions.
- Civil law: based on comprehensive written codes. Originated from Roman law. Used in continental Europe, Latin America, and much of Asia. The Napoleonic Code (1804) is a foundational example.
- Religious law: derived from religious texts. Islamic law (Sharia) is based on the Quran and Hadith. Canon law governs the Catholic Church. Jewish law (Halakha) is based on the Torah and Talmud.
- Customary law: based on long-established practices and traditions of a community. Common in parts of Africa and indigenous communities worldwide.

## Branches of Law

- Constitutional law: defines the structure of government and fundamental rights
- Criminal law: defines offenses against society and prescribes punishments
- Civil law (private law): governs disputes between individuals (contracts, property, torts)
- Administrative law: governs the activities of government agencies
- International law: governs relations between nations (treaties, human rights, trade)
- Environmental law: regulates human impact on the natural environment

## Fundamental Legal Principles

- Rule of law: everyone is subject to the law, including the government
- Presumption of innocence: accused persons are innocent until proven guilty
- Due process: fair procedures must be followed before depriving someone of life, liberty, or property
- Habeas corpus: protection against unlawful detention
- Double jeopardy: a person cannot be tried twice for the same offense
- Statute of limitations: time limits for bringing legal actions

## International Human Rights

The Universal Declaration of Human Rights (1948) established fundamental rights including:

- Right to life, liberty, and security of person
- Freedom from torture and slavery
- Right to a fair trial
- Freedom of thought, conscience, and religion
- Freedom of expression
- Right to education
- Right to work and equal pay for equal work

## How Courts Work

In common law systems, courts are organized hierarchically:

- Trial courts: hear cases first, determine facts, apply law
- Appellate courts: review trial court decisions for legal errors
- Supreme/highest court: final authority on legal interpretation
- Jury system: citizens determine facts in criminal and some civil cases
