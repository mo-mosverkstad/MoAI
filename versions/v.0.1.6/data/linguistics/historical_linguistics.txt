# Historical Linguistics

## Definition

Historical linguistics studies how languages change over time. It reconstructs earlier stages of languages, establishes genetic relationships between languages, and identifies the mechanisms of language change.

## The Comparative Method

The primary tool for establishing language relationships:

1. Identify potential cognates: words in different languages that appear similar in form and meaning
2. Establish systematic sound correspondences between languages
3. Reconstruct proto-forms: the ancestral words from which cognates descended
4. Build a family tree showing how languages diverged

Example: English "father," German "Vater," Latin "pater," Sanskrit "pitar" — all descend from Proto-Indo-European *ph₂tḗr.

## Sound Change

Sound changes are regular — they affect all instances of a sound in the same environment:

- Grimm's Law (1822): systematic consonant shift from Proto-Indo-European to Germanic languages (p→f, t→θ, k→h)
- Great Vowel Shift (1400-1700): English long vowels shifted upward (name was pronounced "nah-meh," bite was "bee-teh")
- Lenition: consonants weaken over time (Latin aqua → Spanish agua → French eau)
- Assimilation: sounds become more similar to neighboring sounds (Latin impossibilis from in- + possibilis)

## Language Families

Major language families established through the comparative method:

- Indo-European: English, Hindi, Russian, Greek, Persian — descended from a language spoken c. 4500 BC
- Sino-Tibetan: Chinese, Tibetan, Burmese
- Austronesian: Malay, Hawaiian, Malagasy — spread across the Pacific
- Afroasiatic: Arabic, Hebrew, Amharic, Somali
- Uralic: Finnish, Hungarian, Estonian

## Proto-Indo-European

The reconstructed ancestor of the Indo-European family, spoken approximately 4500-2500 BC, probably in the Pontic-Caspian steppe. No written records exist, but linguists have reconstructed much of its vocabulary, grammar, and sound system through systematic comparison of daughter languages.

## Dating Language Change

- Glottochronology: estimates divergence dates based on rates of vocabulary replacement (controversial)
- Written records: earliest texts provide firm dates for language states
- Archaeological correlation: linking language spread to material culture
