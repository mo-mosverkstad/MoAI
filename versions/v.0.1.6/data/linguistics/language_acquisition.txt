# Language Acquisition

## Definition

Language acquisition is the process by which humans learn language. First language acquisition occurs naturally in childhood without formal instruction, while second language acquisition typically requires conscious effort.

## First Language Acquisition

Children acquire their native language through predictable stages:

- Cooing (2-4 months): vowel-like sounds
- Babbling (6-8 months): consonant-vowel combinations (ba-ba, da-da)
- First words (10-14 months): single words, often nouns (mama, ball, dog)
- Two-word stage (18-24 months): simple combinations ("more milk," "daddy go")
- Telegraphic speech (2-3 years): short sentences without function words ("cat sit chair")
- Complex sentences (3-5 years): questions, negation, relative clauses, past tense

## Critical Period Hypothesis

Eric Lenneberg (1967) proposed that there is a critical period for language acquisition, roughly from birth to puberty. After this period, acquiring native-like proficiency becomes extremely difficult. Evidence includes:

- Feral children (e.g., Genie) who were isolated from language never fully acquired grammar
- Deaf children exposed to sign language late show reduced grammatical ability
- Second language learners rarely achieve native-like pronunciation after puberty

## Theories of Acquisition

- Nativist (Chomsky): children are born with an innate Language Acquisition Device (LAD) containing Universal Grammar
- Behaviorist (Skinner): language is learned through imitation, reinforcement, and conditioning
- Interactionist (Bruner): language develops through social interaction and scaffolding by caregivers
- Usage-based (Tomasello): children learn language from patterns in the input they hear

## Second Language Acquisition

Key factors affecting success:

- Age of onset: earlier is generally better for pronunciation and grammar
- Motivation: integrative (desire to join a community) and instrumental (career goals)
- Input: quantity and quality of exposure to the target language
- Transfer: first language influences second language learning (positive and negative)
- Individual differences: aptitude, learning strategies, personality
