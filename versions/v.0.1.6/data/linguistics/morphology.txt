# Morphology

## Definition

Morphology is the study of the internal structure of words — how words are formed from smaller meaningful units called morphemes. It examines word formation processes and the rules governing them.

## Morphemes

A morpheme is the smallest unit of meaning in a language:

- Free morphemes: can stand alone as words (cat, run, happy)
- Bound morphemes: must attach to another morpheme (un-, -ing, -s, -ness)
- Prefixes: attach before the root (un-happy, re-write, pre-view)
- Suffixes: attach after the root (happi-ness, teach-er, walk-ed)
- Infixes: inserted within a word (rare in English, common in Tagalog)

## Word Formation Processes

- Derivation: creating new words by adding affixes (happy → unhappiness)
- Compounding: combining two free morphemes (blackboard, sunflower, earthquake)
- Blending: combining parts of two words (smoke + fog = smog, breakfast + lunch = brunch)
- Clipping: shortening a word (advertisement → ad, telephone → phone)
- Acronyms: words formed from initial letters (NASA, scuba, laser)
- Back-formation: creating a new word by removing an affix (editor → edit, television → televise)
- Borrowing: adopting words from other languages (sushi, kindergarten, piano)

## Inflection vs. Derivation

- Inflection: changes grammatical properties without creating a new word (walk/walks/walked/walking)
- Derivation: creates a new word with different meaning or category (teach → teacher, happy → unhappy)

## Morphological Typology

Languages differ in how much information they pack into words:

- Isolating languages (Chinese): one morpheme per word, no inflection
- Agglutinative languages (Turkish, Finnish): words built from many clearly separable morphemes
- Fusional languages (Latin, Russian): morphemes fuse together, one affix carries multiple meanings
- Polysynthetic languages (Inuktitut): entire sentences expressed as single complex words
