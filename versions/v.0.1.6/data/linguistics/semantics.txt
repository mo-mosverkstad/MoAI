# Semantics

## Definition

Semantics is the study of meaning in language. It examines how words, phrases, and sentences convey meaning, and how meaning is composed from smaller units.

## Types of Meaning

- Denotation: the literal, dictionary meaning of a word ("dog" = a four-legged canine animal)
- Connotation: the emotional or cultural associations of a word ("home" suggests warmth and comfort)
- Reference: the relationship between a word and the thing it refers to in the world

## Semantic Relations

Words relate to each other in systematic ways:

- Synonymy: words with similar meanings (big/large, happy/glad)
- Antonymy: words with opposite meanings (hot/cold, alive/dead)
- Hyponymy: one word is a subtype of another (rose is a hyponym of flower)
- Meronymy: part-whole relationships (wheel is a meronym of car)
- Polysemy: one word with multiple related meanings (bank = riverbank or financial institution)
- Homonymy: different words that sound the same (bat = animal or sports equipment)

## Compositional Semantics

The meaning of a sentence is built from the meanings of its parts:

- The principle of compositionality (Frege): the meaning of a complex expression is determined by the meanings of its parts and how they are combined
- Predicate logic: formal representation of sentence meaning using predicates and arguments
- Thematic roles: the semantic relationships between verbs and their arguments (agent, patient, instrument)

## Pragmatics vs. Semantics

Semantics deals with literal meaning; pragmatics deals with meaning in context. "Can you pass the salt?" semantically asks about ability, but pragmatically functions as a request.
