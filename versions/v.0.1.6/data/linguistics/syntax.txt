# Syntax

## Definition

Syntax is the study of how words combine to form phrases, clauses, and sentences. It examines the rules that govern sentence structure in natural languages.

## Sentence Structure

Most languages organize sentences around a basic word order:

- SVO (Subject-Verb-Object): English, Chinese, Spanish — "The cat chased the mouse"
- SOV (Subject-Object-Verb): Japanese, Korean, Hindi — "The cat the mouse chased"
- VSO (Verb-Subject-Object): Arabic, Irish, Welsh — "Chased the cat the mouse"

## Phrase Structure

Sentences are built from hierarchical phrases:

- Noun phrase (NP): a noun and its modifiers ("the big red ball")
- Verb phrase (VP): a verb and its complements ("quickly ate the cake")
- Prepositional phrase (PP): a preposition and its object ("in the garden")
- Adjective phrase (AP): an adjective and its modifiers ("very tall")

## Transformations

Noam Chomsky proposed that sentences have deep structure (meaning) and surface structure (form). Transformational rules convert deep structures into surface structures:

- Passive transformation: "The dog bit the man" → "The man was bitten by the dog"
- Question formation: "She is happy" → "Is she happy?"
- Relativization: "The man who I saw left"

## Universal Grammar

Chomsky argued that all humans are born with an innate language faculty — Universal Grammar — that constrains the possible forms human languages can take. This explains why children acquire language rapidly despite limited input.
