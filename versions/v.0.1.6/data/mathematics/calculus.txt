# Calculus

## Definition

Calculus is a branch of mathematics that studies continuous change. It was independently developed by Isaac Newton and Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz in the late 17th century. Calculus has two major branches: differential calculus and integral calculus.

## Differential Calculus

Differential calculus concerns the study of rates of change and slopes of curves. The derivative of a function measures how the function's output changes as its input changes. The derivative of f(x) is written as f'(x) or df/dx.

Key concepts in differential calculus include:

- Limits: the value a function approaches as the input approaches a certain point
- Continuity: a function is continuous if it has no breaks, jumps, or holes
- Chain rule: used to differentiate composite functions, (f∘g)'(x) = f'(g(x)) · g'(x)
- Product rule: (fg)' = f'g + fg'
- Quotient rule: (f/g)' = (f'g - fg') / g²

## Integral Calculus

Integral calculus is concerned with accumulation of quantities and the areas under and between curves. The definite integral of a function from a to b gives the signed area between the function and the x-axis.

The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus connects differentiation and integration, stating that differentiation and integration are inverse operations. If F is an antiderivative of f, then the integral of f from a to b equals F(b) - F(a).

## Applications of Calculus

Calculus is used extensively in science, engineering, and economics:

- Physics: calculating velocity, acceleration, work, and energy
- Engineering: optimization of designs, signal processing
- Economics: marginal cost and revenue analysis
- Biology: population growth models
- Medicine: modeling drug concentration in the bloodstream over time

## History

Newton developed his method of fluxions around 1666, while Leibniz published his work on calculus in 1684. The notation used today (dx, ∫) was introduced by Leibniz. The rigorous foundation of calculus using epsilon-delta definitions was established by Augustin-Louis Cauchy and Karl Weierstrass in the 19th century.
