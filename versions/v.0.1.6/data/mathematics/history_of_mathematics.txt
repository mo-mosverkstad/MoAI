# History of Mathematics

## Ancient Mathematics

- Babylonians (2000 BC): base-60 number system (still used for time and angles), quadratic equations, Pythagorean triples
- Egyptians (1650 BC): Rhind Papyrus shows arithmetic, fractions, geometry for construction and surveying
- Greeks (600 BC - 300 AD): deductive proof, Euclid's Elements, Archimedes' methods, conic sections
- Indians (500-1200 AD): zero as a number (Brahmagupta, 628 AD), decimal place-value system, negative numbers, early algebra
- Chinese: independent development of the Pythagorean theorem, magic squares, remainder theorem
- Islamic Golden Age (800-1400): algebra (al-Khwarizmi), trigonometry, algorithms, preservation and transmission of Greek mathematics

## Key Milestones

- 300 BC: Euclid's Elements — axiomatic geometry, remained the standard for 2,000 years
- 628 AD: Brahmagupta defines zero and rules for arithmetic with zero and negative numbers
- 820 AD: Al-Khwarizmi's Al-Jabr — founding text of algebra (the word "algebra" derives from the title)
- 1202: Fibonacci introduces Hindu-Arabic numerals to Europe (Liber Abaci)
- 1637: Descartes' coordinate geometry unifies algebra and geometry
- 1665-1666: Newton develops calculus (independently by Leibniz, 1684)
- 1799: Gauss proves the Fundamental Theorem of Algebra
- 1820s: Non-Euclidean geometry discovered (Lobachevsky, Bolyai)
- 1874: Cantor creates set theory, proves different sizes of infinity
- 1931: Gödel's incompleteness theorems — any consistent formal system has true but unprovable statements
- 1994: Andrew Wiles proves Fermat's Last Theorem (after 358 years)

## Great Mathematicians

- Euclid (c. 300 BC): systematized geometry in the Elements
- Archimedes (287-212 BC): calculated pi, discovered principles of buoyancy and levers
- Isaac Newton (1643-1727): calculus, laws of motion, universal gravitation
- Leonhard Euler (1707-1783): most prolific mathematician in history, contributed to every branch
- Carl Friedrich Gauss (1777-1855): "Prince of Mathematicians," number theory, statistics, geometry
- Bernhard Riemann (1826-1866): Riemannian geometry (foundation of general relativity), Riemann hypothesis
- Emmy Noether (1882-1935): abstract algebra, Noether's theorem connecting symmetry and conservation laws
- Srinivasa Ramanujan (1887-1920): self-taught Indian genius, extraordinary results in number theory
- Alan Turing (1912-1954): foundations of computer science, computability theory

## Unsolved Problems

The Clay Mathematics Institute's Millennium Prize Problems ($1 million each):

- Riemann Hypothesis (number theory)
- P vs NP (computational complexity)
- Navier-Stokes existence and smoothness (fluid dynamics)
- Yang-Mills existence and mass gap (quantum physics)
- Birch and Swinnerton-Dyer conjecture (number theory)
- Hodge conjecture (algebraic geometry)
- Poincaré conjecture (topology) — SOLVED by Grigori Perelman (2003)
