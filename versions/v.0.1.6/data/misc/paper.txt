# Paper

## History

Paper is one of the most important inventions in human history, enabling the recording and transmission of knowledge across generations.

- 105 AD: Cai Lun in China standardizes papermaking from bark, hemp, rags, and fishnets
- 751 AD: papermaking knowledge reaches the Islamic world after the Battle of Talas
- 1150: first paper mill in Europe (Xàtiva, Spain)
- 1450: Gutenberg's printing press creates massive demand for paper
- 1799: Louis-Nicolas Robert invents the continuous papermaking machine
- 1840s: wood pulp replaces rags as the primary raw material

## How Paper is Made

Modern papermaking process:

1. Pulping: wood chips are broken down into fibers using mechanical or chemical processes
2. Bleaching: pulp is whitened using chlorine dioxide or oxygen-based chemicals
3. Beating/refining: fibers are processed to improve bonding
4. Forming: dilute pulp suspension is spread on a moving wire mesh, water drains away
5. Pressing: wet sheet passes through rollers to remove more water
6. Drying: heated cylinders evaporate remaining moisture
7. Finishing: calendering (smoothing), coating, or sizing for specific uses

## Types of Paper

- Newsprint: lightweight, inexpensive, made from mechanical pulp
- Bond paper: high-quality writing and printing paper
- Cardboard/paperboard: thick, rigid, used for packaging
- Tissue paper: soft, absorbent (toilet paper, facial tissue)
- Art paper: coated for high-quality image reproduction
- Rice paper: thin, translucent, used in Asian art and calligraphy
- Watercolor paper: heavy, textured, absorbs water without warping

## Environmental Impact

- Paper industry accounts for approximately 4% of global energy use
- Recycling one ton of paper saves 17 trees, 26,000 liters of water, and 2.5 cubic meters of landfill space
- Recycled paper requires 70% less energy than virgin paper
- Sustainable forestry certification (FSC) ensures responsible sourcing
- Digital alternatives reduce but have not eliminated paper demand

## Origami

Origami is the Japanese art of paper folding (ori = folding, kami = paper):

- Traditional origami uses a single square sheet without cutting or gluing
- Classic models: crane (tsuru), boat, frog, butterfly
- Modular origami: multiple folded units assembled into complex structures
- Mathematical origami: explores geometric principles and has applications in engineering
- Applications: solar panel deployment in space, medical stents, airbag folding, architectural design
