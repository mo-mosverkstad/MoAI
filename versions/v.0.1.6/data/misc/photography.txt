# Photography

## Definition

Photography is the art and science of capturing light to create images. The word comes from Greek: photos (light) and graphe (drawing) — literally "drawing with light."

## History

- 1826: Joseph Nicéphore Niépce creates the first permanent photograph (8-hour exposure)
- 1839: Louis Daguerre announces the daguerreotype process (minutes-long exposure on silver-coated copper)
- 1841: William Henry Fox Talbot patents the calotype (negative-positive process enabling multiple prints)
- 1888: George Eastman introduces the Kodak camera ("You press the button, we do the rest")
- 1936: First color slide film (Kodachrome) introduced
- 1975: Steven Sasson at Kodak builds the first digital camera (0.01 megapixels)
- 2000s: Digital cameras surpass film in quality and convenience
- 2007: iPhone launches, making photography ubiquitous (over 1.4 trillion photos taken annually by 2020)

## How Cameras Work

All cameras share basic principles:

- Lens: focuses light onto the recording medium
- Aperture: controls how much light enters (measured in f-stops: f/1.4, f/2.8, f/5.6, etc.)
- Shutter: controls how long light hits the sensor (1/1000s to several seconds)
- Sensor (or film): records the light pattern as an image
- ISO/sensitivity: amplifies the signal (higher ISO = more sensitive but more noise)

The exposure triangle: aperture, shutter speed, and ISO work together to determine exposure.

## Types of Photography

- Portrait: capturing people, emphasizing expression and personality
- Landscape: natural scenery, often using wide-angle lenses and small apertures
- Street photography: candid images of everyday life in public spaces
- Wildlife: animals in natural habitats, requiring telephoto lenses and patience
- Macro: extreme close-ups revealing details invisible to the naked eye
- Astrophotography: celestial objects (stars, galaxies, nebulae)
- Documentary/photojournalism: recording events and social conditions

## Composition Principles

- Rule of thirds: placing subjects at intersections of a 3×3 grid
- Leading lines: using lines to guide the viewer's eye through the image
- Framing: using elements in the scene to frame the subject
- Depth of field: controlling what is sharp and what is blurred
- Light: the quality, direction, and color of light define the mood
