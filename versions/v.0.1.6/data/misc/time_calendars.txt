# Time and Calendars

## Measuring Time

Humans have measured time using natural cycles and increasingly precise instruments:

- Sundials (1500 BC): shadow position indicates time of day
- Water clocks (1400 BC): measured time by regulated water flow
- Mechanical clocks (1300 AD): weight-driven mechanisms with escapements
- Pendulum clocks (1656): Christiaan Huygens, accurate to 15 seconds per day
- Quartz clocks (1927): vibrating quartz crystal, accurate to milliseconds per day
- Atomic clocks (1955): cesium-133 atom vibrations, accurate to 1 second in 300 million years

## Time Zones

Before railways, each city set its own local time by the sun. Standard time zones were adopted in 1884 at the International Meridian Conference in Washington, D.C.

- 24 time zones, each approximately 15 degrees of longitude wide
- Greenwich Mean Time (GMT) / Coordinated Universal Time (UTC) is the reference
- International Date Line: roughly follows 180° longitude in the Pacific Ocean
- Some countries use half-hour or quarter-hour offsets (India: UTC+5:30, Nepal: UTC+5:45)
- Daylight Saving Time: clocks advanced 1 hour in summer (controversial, being abolished in some regions)

## Calendars

- Egyptian calendar (3000 BC): 365 days, 12 months of 30 days plus 5 extra days
- Julian calendar (45 BC): introduced by Julius Caesar, 365.25 days (leap year every 4 years)
- Gregorian calendar (1582): corrected the Julian calendar's drift (omits leap years in most century years). Used worldwide today.
- Islamic calendar: lunar, 354 or 355 days, months begin with the new moon crescent
- Chinese calendar: lunisolar, basis for Chinese New Year and traditional festivals
- Hebrew calendar: lunisolar, used for Jewish religious observances

## The Second

The second is the base unit of time in the International System of Units (SI):

- Originally defined as 1/86,400 of a mean solar day
- Since 1967: defined as 9,192,631,770 oscillations of the cesium-133 atom
- Leap seconds: occasionally added to UTC to keep it synchronized with Earth's slowing rotation
- Proposed redefinition: optical atomic clocks may redefine the second with even greater precision

## Deep Time

- Age of the universe: approximately 13.8 billion years
- Age of Earth: approximately 4.54 billion years
- Age of life on Earth: approximately 3.7 billion years
- Age of modern humans: approximately 300,000 years
- Recorded history: approximately 5,000 years
