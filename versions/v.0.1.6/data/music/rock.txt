# Rock Music

## Origins

Rock and roll emerged in the United States in the late 1940s and early 1950s, evolving from African American musical genres including rhythm and blues, gospel, and jazz, combined with country music. The term "rock and roll" was popularized by radio DJ Alan Freed in 1951.

## Pioneers

- Chuck Berry (1926-2017): defined rock guitar style with songs like "Johnny B. Goode"
- Elvis Presley (1935-1977): "The King of Rock and Roll," brought rock to mainstream white audiences
- Little Richard (1932-2020): flamboyant performer whose energy defined early rock
- Buddy Holly (1936-1959): singer-songwriter who influenced the Beatles and many others
- Fats Domino (1928-2017): New Orleans pianist who sold over 65 million records

## Major Subgenres

- British Invasion (1960s): Beatles, Rolling Stones, The Who, The Kinks
- Psychedelic rock (late 1960s): Jimi Hendrix, Pink Floyd, Jefferson Airplane
- Hard rock (1970s): Led Zeppelin, Deep Purple, AC/DC
- Punk rock (1976-): Ramones, Sex Pistols, The Clash — raw, fast, anti-establishment
- Progressive rock (1970s): Yes, Genesis, King Crimson — complex compositions, concept albums
- Heavy metal (1970s-): Black Sabbath, Iron Maiden, Metallica — distorted guitars, powerful vocals
- Alternative rock (1980s-1990s): R.E.M., Nirvana, Radiohead
- Grunge (early 1990s): Nirvana, Pearl Jam, Soundgarden — Seattle scene

## The Beatles

The Beatles (1960-1970) are widely regarded as the most influential rock band in history. John Lennon, Paul McCartney, George Harrison, and Ringo Starr transformed popular music through constant innovation, from early pop ("She Loves You") through psychedelia ("Strawberry Fields Forever") to experimental works ("Revolution 9"). They sold over 600 million records worldwide.

## Cultural Impact

Rock music has been a vehicle for social change:

- Civil rights movement: integration of black and white musical traditions
- Anti-war movement: protest songs during Vietnam era
- Youth counterculture: challenging authority and social norms
- Fashion: from leather jackets to bell-bottoms to punk safety pins
- Technology: electric guitar, amplification, studio recording techniques
