# Eastern Philosophy

## Overview

Eastern philosophy encompasses the philosophical traditions of Asia, including Indian, Chinese, and Japanese systems. These traditions often integrate philosophy with spiritual practice and emphasize harmony, balance, and the nature of self.

## Indian Philosophy

- Hinduism: the Upanishads teach that Atman (individual self) is identical with Brahman (ultimate reality). The goal is moksha — liberation from the cycle of rebirth (samsara). Key texts: Vedas, Upanishads, Bhagavad Gita.
- Buddhism: founded by Siddhartha Gautama (the Buddha, c. 500 BC). Four Noble Truths: life involves suffering (dukkha); suffering arises from craving; suffering can end; the Eightfold Path leads to its end. Key concepts: impermanence (anicca), non-self (anatta), dependent origination.
- Jainism: emphasizes non-violence (ahimsa), truth, and asceticism. All living beings have souls (jiva). Liberation through right faith, right knowledge, and right conduct.
- Yoga philosophy: Patanjali's Yoga Sutras describe eight limbs of practice leading to samadhi (absorption). Yoga means "union" — of individual consciousness with universal consciousness.

## Chinese Philosophy

- Confucianism (Kongzi, 551-479 BC): emphasizes social harmony, filial piety, ritual propriety (li), and benevolence (ren). The ideal is the junzi (exemplary person). Five relationships: ruler-subject, parent-child, husband-wife, elder-younger, friend-friend.
- Daoism (Laozi, Zhuangzi): living in harmony with the Dao (the Way) — the natural order of the universe. Wu wei (non-action): effortless action aligned with nature. "The Dao that can be told is not the eternal Dao."
- Legalism (Han Feizi): human nature is selfish; strict laws and punishments are necessary for social order. Influenced the Qin dynasty's governance.
- Neo-Confucianism (Zhu Xi, Wang Yangming): synthesis of Confucian ethics with Buddhist and Daoist metaphysics during the Song dynasty.

## Japanese Philosophy

- Zen Buddhism: emphasizes zazen (sitting meditation), koans (paradoxical riddles), and satori (sudden enlightenment). "What is the sound of one hand clapping?"
- Bushido: the way of the warrior — loyalty, honor, self-discipline, acceptance of death
- Wabi-sabi: aesthetic appreciation of imperfection, impermanence, and incompleteness
- Mono no aware: sensitivity to the transience of things, bittersweet awareness of passing beauty

## Key Differences from Western Philosophy

- Less emphasis on logical argumentation, more on direct experience and practice
- Self often seen as illusory or interconnected rather than autonomous
- Harmony and balance valued over individual rights
- Philosophy and spiritual practice often inseparable
- Cyclical rather than linear view of time
- Complementary opposites (yin-yang) rather than strict either/or thinking
