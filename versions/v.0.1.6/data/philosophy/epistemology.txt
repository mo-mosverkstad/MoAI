# Epistemology

## Definition

Epistemology is the branch of philosophy concerned with the nature, sources, and limits of knowledge. It asks: What is knowledge? How do we acquire it? What can we know with certainty?

## The Classical Definition

Knowledge is traditionally defined as justified true belief (JTB):

- Belief: the subject holds the proposition to be true
- Truth: the proposition is actually true
- Justification: the subject has good reasons for the belief

In 1963, Edmund Gettier showed that JTB is insufficient — there are cases where someone has a justified true belief that intuitively is not knowledge (Gettier problems).

## Sources of Knowledge

- Perception: knowledge gained through the five senses
- Reason: knowledge derived through logical deduction (mathematics, logic)
- Testimony: knowledge gained from others (education, books, news)
- Memory: retention of previously acquired knowledge
- Introspection: knowledge of one's own mental states

## Skepticism

Philosophical skepticism questions whether knowledge is possible:

- Cartesian skepticism: Descartes' evil demon — how can we know we're not being systematically deceived?
- Brain in a vat: how can we know we're not brains being fed simulated experiences?
- Problem of induction (Hume): past regularities don't guarantee future ones
- Pyrrhonian skepticism: suspend judgment on all claims, achieving tranquility (ataraxia)

## Rationalism vs. Empiricism

- Rationalism (Descartes, Leibniz, Spinoza): reason is the primary source of knowledge. Some knowledge is innate or a priori (independent of experience). "I think, therefore I am" (cogito ergo sum).
- Empiricism (Locke, Hume, Berkeley): all knowledge comes from sensory experience. The mind begins as a blank slate (tabula rasa). No innate ideas.
- Kant's synthesis: some knowledge is a priori but structured by the mind's categories (space, time, causality). We can know the world as it appears to us (phenomena) but not as it is in itself (noumena).

## Contemporary Epistemology

- Reliabilism: a belief is justified if produced by a reliable cognitive process
- Virtue epistemology: intellectual virtues (open-mindedness, thoroughness) constitute good epistemic practice
- Social epistemology: how social factors affect knowledge (testimony, expertise, group belief)
- Formal epistemology: using probability theory and logic to model rational belief
- Epistemic injustice (Fricker): wrongly denying someone's credibility due to prejudice
