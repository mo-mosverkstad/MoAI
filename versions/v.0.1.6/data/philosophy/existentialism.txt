# Existentialism

## Overview

Existentialism is a philosophical movement emphasizing individual existence, freedom, and choice. It holds that humans define their own meaning in life and must confront the inherent absurdity and anxiety of existence. The movement flourished in 20th-century Europe, particularly post-war France.

## Key Themes

- Existence precedes essence: humans have no predetermined nature; we create ourselves through choices
- Radical freedom: we are "condemned to be free" — every moment requires choice, and we cannot escape responsibility
- Authenticity: living according to one's own values rather than conforming to social expectations
- Absurdity: the conflict between humans' desire for meaning and the universe's apparent meaninglessness
- Anxiety (Angst): dread accompanying awareness of our freedom and mortality
- Bad faith: self-deception, denying one's freedom by pretending to be determined by external forces

## Major Thinkers

- Søren Kierkegaard (1813-1855): Danish philosopher, father of existentialism. Emphasized subjective truth and the leap of faith. "Life can only be understood backwards; but it must be lived forwards."
- Friedrich Nietzsche (1844-1900): proclaimed the death of God and the need to create new values. Introduced the Übermensch and eternal recurrence.
- Martin Heidegger (1889-1976): analyzed Being and human existence (Dasein). Explored authenticity and being-toward-death in Being and Time (1927).
- Jean-Paul Sartre (1905-1980): declared "existence precedes essence." Wrote Being and Nothingness. "Man is condemned to be free."
- Simone de Beauvoir (1908-1986): applied existentialism to feminism. "One is not born, but rather becomes, a woman."
- Albert Camus (1913-1960): explored the absurd. "One must imagine Sisyphus happy." The Stranger, The Myth of Sisyphus.

## Existentialism and Literature

- Dostoevsky's Notes from Underground: the underground man rebels against rationalism
- Kafka's The Trial: Joseph K. faces an incomprehensible system
- Sartre's Nausea: Roquentin confronts the contingency of existence
- Camus' The Stranger: Meursault lives without conventional meaning
- Beckett's Waiting for Godot: two men wait endlessly for someone who never comes

## Criticism

- Accused of excessive pessimism and nihilism
- Overemphasis on individual at expense of community and social structures
- Difficulty providing concrete ethical guidance
- Heidegger's association with Nazism complicates the movement's legacy
