# Political Philosophy

## Definition

Political philosophy examines fundamental questions about government, justice, rights, liberty, and the relationship between individuals and the state.

## Social Contract Theory

The idea that political authority derives from agreement among individuals:

- Thomas Hobbes (1588-1679): without government, life would be "solitary, poor, nasty, brutish, and short." People surrender freedoms to a sovereign for security. Leviathan (1651).
- John Locke (1632-1704): government exists to protect natural rights (life, liberty, property). Citizens may overthrow governments that violate these rights. Two Treatises of Government (1689).
- Jean-Jacques Rousseau (1712-1778): legitimate authority rests on the general will. "Man is born free, and everywhere he is in chains." The Social Contract (1762).

## Theories of Justice

- Utilitarianism (Bentham, Mill): just institutions maximize overall welfare
- Libertarianism (Nozick): justice requires respecting individual rights, especially property; minimal state
- Egalitarianism (Rawls): just institutions are those chosen behind a "veil of ignorance." Difference principle: inequalities are just only if they benefit the least advantaged.
- Communitarianism (MacIntyre, Sandel): justice must account for community values and traditions
- Capabilities approach (Sen, Nussbaum): justice requires ensuring people have real freedoms to achieve well-being

## Liberty

- Negative liberty (Berlin): freedom from interference by others
- Positive liberty (Berlin): freedom to achieve one's potential, self-mastery
- Mill's harm principle: the only justification for limiting liberty is to prevent harm to others
- Paradox of tolerance (Popper): unlimited tolerance leads to the disappearance of tolerance

## Forms of Government

- Direct democracy: citizens vote on laws directly (ancient Athens, Swiss referendums)
- Representative democracy: citizens elect representatives who make laws
- Constitutional monarchy: monarch as head of state with limited powers (UK, Sweden, Japan)
- Authoritarianism: concentrated power with limited political freedoms
- Totalitarianism: state controls all aspects of public and private life

## Contemporary Issues

- Global justice: do wealthy nations owe obligations to the global poor?
- Immigration: what rights do states have to control borders?
- Civil disobedience: when is breaking the law morally justified?
- Free speech limits: hate speech, misinformation, platform regulation
- Democratic backsliding: erosion of democratic norms and institutions
