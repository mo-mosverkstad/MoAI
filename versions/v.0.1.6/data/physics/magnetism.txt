# Magnetism

## Definition

Magnetism is a fundamental force of nature arising from the motion of electric charges. It is one aspect of electromagnetism — one of the four fundamental forces. Magnetic fields exert forces on moving charges and magnetic materials.

## Magnetic Fields

- Every magnet has a north pole and a south pole — they cannot be separated (no magnetic monopoles have been found)
- Like poles repel; opposite poles attract
- Magnetic field lines run from north to south outside the magnet
- Earth has a magnetic field generated by convection currents in its liquid iron outer core
- Earth's magnetic field protects life from solar wind and cosmic radiation

## Sources of Magnetism

- Permanent magnets: materials with aligned magnetic domains (iron, nickel, cobalt, neodymium alloys)
- Electromagnets: magnetic field produced by electric current flowing through a coil. Strength controlled by current. Used in motors, generators, MRI machines, and particle accelerators.
- Earth's magnetic field: generated by the geodynamo — convection of liquid iron in the outer core. Strength: 25-65 microtesla at the surface.

## Electromagnetism

James Clerk Maxwell (1865) unified electricity and magnetism into a single theory:

- A changing electric field produces a magnetic field
- A changing magnetic field produces an electric field
- Light is an electromagnetic wave (oscillating electric and magnetic fields)
- Maxwell's equations: four equations that describe all electromagnetic phenomena

## Applications

- Electric motors: convert electrical energy to mechanical energy using magnetic forces
- Generators: convert mechanical energy to electrical energy (reverse of motors)
- Transformers: change voltage levels using electromagnetic induction
- MRI (Magnetic Resonance Imaging): strong magnetic fields align hydrogen atoms in the body for medical imaging
- Magnetic storage: hard drives store data as magnetic patterns on spinning disks
- Maglev trains: magnetic levitation eliminates friction for high-speed rail (Shanghai Maglev: 431 km/h)
- Particle accelerators: magnetic fields steer charged particles (Large Hadron Collider uses 1,232 superconducting magnets)

## Types of Magnetic Materials

- Ferromagnetic: strongly attracted to magnets, can be permanently magnetized (iron, nickel, cobalt)
- Paramagnetic: weakly attracted to magnets (aluminum, platinum, oxygen)
- Diamagnetic: weakly repelled by magnets (copper, water, carbon, bismuth)
- Superconductors: perfectly diamagnetic — completely expel magnetic fields (Meissner effect), enabling magnetic levitation
