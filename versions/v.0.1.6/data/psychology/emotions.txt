# Emotions

## Definition

Emotions are complex psychological states involving three components: a subjective experience (feeling), a physiological response (bodily changes), and a behavioral expression (actions or facial expressions). Emotions evolved to help organisms respond adaptively to important environmental events.

## Basic Emotions

Paul Ekman (1972) identified six universal basic emotions recognized across all cultures through facial expressions:

- Happiness: signaled by smiling, associated with reward and social bonding
- Sadness: signaled by frowning and tears, associated with loss
- Fear: signaled by wide eyes and open mouth, associated with threat detection
- Anger: signaled by furrowed brows and clenched jaw, associated with goal obstruction
- Disgust: signaled by wrinkled nose, associated with contamination avoidance
- Surprise: signaled by raised eyebrows, associated with unexpected events

## Theories of Emotion

- James-Lange theory (1884): we feel emotions because of bodily changes (we feel sad because we cry)
- Cannon-Bard theory (1927): bodily changes and emotional experience occur simultaneously
- Schachter-Singer two-factor theory (1962): emotion = physiological arousal + cognitive label
- Appraisal theory (Lazarus): emotions result from how we evaluate (appraise) events
- Constructionist theory (Barrett): emotions are constructed from basic affect (valence + arousal) plus conceptual knowledge

## The Neuroscience of Emotion

- Amygdala: rapid threat detection, fear conditioning, emotional memory
- Prefrontal cortex: emotion regulation, decision-making involving emotions
- Insula: interoception (awareness of bodily states), disgust
- Anterior cingulate cortex: conflict monitoring, emotional pain
- Ventral striatum: reward processing, pleasure

## Emotional Intelligence

Daniel Goleman (1995) popularized the concept of emotional intelligence (EQ):

- Self-awareness: recognizing one's own emotions
- Self-regulation: managing emotions effectively
- Motivation: using emotions to drive goal pursuit
- Empathy: understanding others' emotions
- Social skills: managing relationships effectively

## Emotion Regulation

Strategies for managing emotions:

- Cognitive reappraisal: reinterpreting a situation to change its emotional impact
- Suppression: inhibiting emotional expression (less effective, more costly)
- Mindfulness: observing emotions without judgment
- Distraction: redirecting attention away from emotional stimuli
- Problem-solving: addressing the source of negative emotions directly
