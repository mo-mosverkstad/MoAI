# Learning and Conditioning

## Definition

Learning is a relatively permanent change in behavior or knowledge resulting from experience. Psychology identifies several distinct mechanisms through which learning occurs.

## Classical Conditioning

Discovered by Ivan Pavlov (1890s) through experiments with dogs:

- Unconditioned stimulus (US): naturally triggers a response (food causes salivation)
- Unconditioned response (UR): natural reaction to the US (salivation)
- Conditioned stimulus (CS): neutral stimulus paired with the US (bell)
- Conditioned response (CR): learned reaction to the CS (salivation to bell alone)

Key phenomena:

- Acquisition: the CS-US pairing is learned
- Extinction: the CR weakens when CS is presented without US
- Spontaneous recovery: extinguished CR reappears after a rest period
- Generalization: similar stimuli also trigger the CR
- Discrimination: learning to respond only to the specific CS

## Operant Conditioning

B.F. Skinner (1930s-1960s) studied how consequences shape behavior:

- Positive reinforcement: adding something pleasant increases behavior (praise, rewards)
- Negative reinforcement: removing something unpleasant increases behavior (taking aspirin removes headache)
- Positive punishment: adding something unpleasant decreases behavior (spanking)
- Negative punishment: removing something pleasant decreases behavior (taking away privileges)

Schedules of reinforcement:

- Fixed ratio: reinforcement after a set number of responses (every 5th response)
- Variable ratio: reinforcement after an unpredictable number (slot machines)
- Fixed interval: reinforcement for first response after a set time period
- Variable interval: reinforcement after unpredictable time periods

## Observational Learning

Albert Bandura (1961) demonstrated that people learn by watching others:

- Bobo doll experiment: children who watched adults act aggressively toward a doll imitated the aggression
- Modeling: learning by observing and imitating a model's behavior
- Vicarious reinforcement: learning from observing consequences others receive
- Self-efficacy: belief in one's ability to succeed, influenced by observing similar others succeed
