# Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD)

## Definition

Post-traumatic stress disorder is a mental health condition triggered by experiencing or witnessing a terrifying event. While it is normal to have temporary difficulty coping after trauma, PTSD occurs when symptoms persist for months or years and significantly impair daily functioning.

## Causes and Risk Factors

Events that can trigger PTSD:

- Combat and military service
- Sexual assault or abuse
- Physical violence or assault
- Serious accidents (car crashes, industrial accidents)
- Natural disasters (earthquakes, floods, hurricanes)
- Terrorist attacks
- Childhood abuse or neglect
- Witnessing death or serious injury

Risk factors: prior trauma, family history of mental illness, lack of social support, severity and duration of trauma, female sex (twice the risk of males).

## Symptoms

PTSD symptoms fall into four clusters:

- Intrusion: flashbacks (reliving the trauma), nightmares, intrusive memories, intense distress at reminders
- Avoidance: avoiding thoughts, feelings, places, people, or activities associated with the trauma
- Negative alterations in cognition and mood: persistent negative beliefs, emotional numbness, detachment from others, inability to experience positive emotions, distorted blame
- Arousal and reactivity: hypervigilance, exaggerated startle response, irritability, difficulty concentrating, sleep disturbance, reckless behavior

## Prevalence

- Approximately 6% of the US population will have PTSD at some point in their lives
- About 15-30% of combat veterans develop PTSD
- Women are approximately twice as likely as men to develop PTSD
- Most people exposed to trauma do NOT develop PTSD (resilience is the norm)

## Neurobiology

- Amygdala: hyperactive, triggering exaggerated fear responses
- Prefrontal cortex: underactive, reducing ability to regulate fear
- Hippocampus: reduced volume, impairing contextualization of memories
- HPA axis: dysregulated stress hormone (cortisol) response
- Norepinephrine system: elevated, contributing to hyperarousal

## Treatment

- Cognitive Processing Therapy (CPT): identifying and challenging trauma-related thoughts
- Prolonged Exposure (PE): gradually confronting trauma memories and avoided situations
- EMDR (Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing): processing traumatic memories while following bilateral stimulation
- Medications: SSRIs (sertraline, paroxetine) are first-line pharmacotherapy
- Group therapy: connecting with others who share similar experiences
- Stellate ganglion block: emerging treatment showing promise for hyperarousal symptoms
