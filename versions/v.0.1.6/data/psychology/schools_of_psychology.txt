# Schools of Psychology

## Structuralism (1879)

Founded by Wilhelm Wundt at the University of Leipzig — the first psychology laboratory.

- Goal: identify the basic elements (structures) of conscious experience
- Method: introspection — trained observers reporting their conscious experiences
- Key figure: Edward Titchener brought structuralism to America
- Limitation: introspection proved unreliable — different observers reported different experiences
- Legacy: established psychology as an experimental science separate from philosophy

## Functionalism (1890s)

Founded by William James at Harvard University.

- Goal: understand the purpose (function) of mental processes — why we think, feel, and behave
- Influenced by Darwin's theory of evolution — mental processes evolved because they are adaptive
- Method: observation of behavior in natural settings, not just laboratory introspection
- Key figures: William James, John Dewey, James Rowland Angell
- Legacy: led to applied psychology, educational psychology, and developmental psychology

## Psychoanalysis (1890s-1930s)

Founded by Sigmund Freud in Vienna.

- Goal: understand the unconscious mind and its influence on behavior
- Key concepts: unconscious motivation, defense mechanisms, psychosexual development, dream analysis, free association
- Method: clinical case studies, free association, dream interpretation
- Key figures: Freud, Carl Jung (collective unconscious, archetypes), Alfred Adler (inferiority complex), Anna Freud (ego psychology)
- Criticism: unfalsifiable theories, overemphasis on sexuality, based on small clinical samples
- Legacy: talk therapy, recognition of unconscious processes, influence on art and culture

## Behaviorism (1913-1960s)

Founded by John B. Watson; developed by B.F. Skinner.

- Goal: study only observable, measurable behavior — reject the study of consciousness
- Key principle: behavior is shaped by environment through conditioning
- Method: controlled laboratory experiments with animals and humans
- Key figures: Watson (Little Albert experiment), Skinner (operant conditioning, Skinner box), Pavlov (classical conditioning)
- Criticism: ignores mental processes, oversimplifies human behavior, ethical concerns with animal research
- Legacy: behavior therapy, applied behavior analysis, evidence-based treatment

## Gestalt Psychology (1912)

Founded by Max Wertheimer, Kurt Koffka, and Wolfgang Köhler in Germany.

- Goal: understand how the mind organizes perceptual experience into meaningful wholes
- Key principle: "The whole is different from the sum of its parts"
- Contributions: laws of perceptual organization (proximity, similarity, closure, continuity)
- Method: phenomenological observation of perceptual experience
- Legacy: influenced cognitive psychology, design principles, problem-solving research

## Humanistic Psychology (1950s-1960s)

Founded by Abraham Maslow and Carl Rogers — the "Third Force" (after psychoanalysis and behaviorism).

- Goal: understand human potential, growth, and self-actualization
- Key principles: free will, subjective experience, inherent goodness of people
- Method: qualitative research, case studies, phenomenological approaches
- Key concepts: hierarchy of needs (Maslow), unconditional positive regard (Rogers), client-centered therapy
- Criticism: difficult to test scientifically, overly optimistic view of human nature
- Legacy: positive psychology, person-centered therapy, emphasis on well-being

## Cognitive Psychology (1960s-present)

Emerged as the "cognitive revolution" against behaviorism.

- Goal: understand mental processes — attention, memory, perception, language, problem-solving, decision-making
- Key metaphor: the mind as an information-processing system (computer analogy)
- Method: controlled experiments measuring reaction times, error rates, brain imaging
- Key figures: Ulric Neisser, George Miller, Noam Chomsky, Daniel Kahneman
- Contributions: working memory models, cognitive biases, artificial intelligence
- Legacy: dominant paradigm in modern psychology, cognitive-behavioral therapy

## Evolutionary Psychology (1990s-present)

Applies evolutionary theory to understand human behavior and cognition.

- Goal: explain psychological traits as evolved adaptations to ancestral environments
- Key principle: the mind contains specialized modules shaped by natural selection
- Topics: mate selection, aggression, cooperation, fear, language, parenting
- Key figures: Leda Cosmides, John Tooby, David Buss, Steven Pinker
- Criticism: just-so stories, difficulty testing evolutionary hypotheses, potential for naturalistic fallacy
- Legacy: integration of biology and psychology, understanding of universal human nature

## Positive Psychology (1998-present)

Founded by Martin Seligman.

- Goal: study what makes life worth living — strengths, virtues, well-being, and flourishing
- Shift from fixing pathology to building positive qualities
- Key topics: happiness, gratitude, resilience, flow, meaning, character strengths
- PERMA model (Seligman): Positive emotions, Engagement, Relationships, Meaning, Accomplishment
- Criticism: may neglect genuine suffering, cultural bias toward Western individualism
- Legacy: well-being interventions, strengths-based approaches in education and organizations
