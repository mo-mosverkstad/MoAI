# Stress and Coping

## What is Stress

Stress is the body's response to demands or threats (stressors). Hans Selye (1936) defined stress as "the non-specific response of the body to any demand." Moderate stress can enhance performance, but chronic stress damages physical and mental health.

## The Stress Response

The fight-or-flight response (Walter Cannon, 1915):

1. Threat perceived by the brain (amygdala)
2. Hypothalamus activates the sympathetic nervous system
3. Adrenal glands release adrenaline (epinephrine) and cortisol
4. Heart rate increases, blood pressure rises, breathing quickens
5. Blood flow redirected to muscles, digestion suppressed
6. Pupils dilate, senses sharpen

## General Adaptation Syndrome (Selye)

Three stages of prolonged stress:

1. Alarm: initial fight-or-flight response, body mobilizes resources
2. Resistance: body adapts to ongoing stress, appears to cope but resources are depleting
3. Exhaustion: prolonged stress depletes resources, leading to illness and breakdown

## Types of Stressors

- Acute stressors: short-term threats (exams, job interviews, near-accidents)
- Chronic stressors: ongoing pressures (poverty, caregiving, toxic relationships)
- Daily hassles: minor irritations that accumulate (traffic, deadlines, noise)
- Life events: major changes requiring adaptation (divorce, bereavement, job loss, moving)

## Health Effects of Chronic Stress

- Cardiovascular disease: elevated blood pressure, increased heart attack risk
- Immune suppression: increased susceptibility to infections and slower wound healing
- Mental health: depression, anxiety, burnout
- Digestive problems: irritable bowel syndrome, ulcers
- Cognitive impairment: memory problems, difficulty concentrating
- Accelerated aging: shortened telomeres

## Coping Strategies

- Problem-focused coping: directly addressing the source of stress (planning, seeking information, taking action)
- Emotion-focused coping: managing emotional response to stress (reappraisal, acceptance, humor)
- Social support: seeking help, comfort, and advice from others
- Exercise: reduces cortisol, releases endorphins, improves mood
- Mindfulness meditation: reduces rumination and physiological stress response
- Cognitive-behavioral techniques: identifying and challenging stress-inducing thoughts
