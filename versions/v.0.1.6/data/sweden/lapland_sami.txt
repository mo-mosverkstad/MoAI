# Lapland and the Sami People

## Swedish Lapland

Lapland (Lappland) is the northernmost region of Sweden, stretching above the Arctic Circle. It covers approximately one-quarter of Sweden's total area but has fewer than 100,000 inhabitants. The landscape includes vast boreal forests, mountain ranges, glacial rivers, and tundra.

## The Sami People

The Sami are the indigenous people of northern Scandinavia (Sápmi), with a presence dating back at least 5,000 years. Approximately 20,000-40,000 Sami live in Sweden (total Sami population across Norway, Sweden, Finland, and Russia: 80,000-100,000).

## Sami Culture

- Reindeer herding: the traditional livelihood, still practiced by approximately 2,500 Sami in Sweden. Herders follow seasonal migration routes between coast and mountains.
- Joik: traditional Sami vocal art — a form of singing that evokes a person, animal, or place rather than describing it. One of the oldest musical traditions in Europe.
- Duodji: traditional Sami handicraft using reindeer antler, leather, pewter, and birch wood
- Lavvu: traditional tent similar to a tipi, used during reindeer migration
- Gákti: traditional Sami clothing with distinctive colors and patterns indicating region and family

## Sami Languages

The Sami languages belong to the Uralic language family (related to Finnish and Estonian, not to Swedish):

- Nine distinct Sami languages exist (three spoken in Sweden)
- North Sami: most widely spoken (~20,000 speakers)
- Lule Sami: approximately 2,000 speakers
- South Sami: approximately 500 speakers (endangered)
- All Sami languages are classified as endangered by UNESCO

## Rights and Recognition

- 1977: Swedish Parliament recognizes the Sami as an indigenous people
- 1993: Sami Parliament (Sametinget) established — elected body representing Sami interests
- Reindeer herding rights: legally protected, exclusively for Sami
- Land rights: ongoing disputes over mining, forestry, and wind power on traditional Sami lands
- 2011: Sweden ratifies ILO Convention 169 on indigenous peoples' rights (still debated)

## Tourism

Swedish Lapland attracts visitors for:

- Northern Lights (Aurora Borealis): best viewed September-March
- Midnight Sun: continuous daylight from late May to mid-July above the Arctic Circle
- Icehotel (Jukkasjärvi): rebuilt annually from ice harvested from the Torne River (since 1989)
- Dog sledding and snowmobile tours
- Abisko National Park: renowned for clear skies and aurora viewing
- Kungsleden (King's Trail): 440 km hiking trail through mountain wilderness
