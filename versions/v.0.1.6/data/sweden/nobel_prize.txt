# The Nobel Prize

## Origins

The Nobel Prize was established by the will of Alfred Nobel (1833-1896), a Swedish chemist, engineer, and inventor who held 355 patents, including the patent for dynamite. Troubled that his invention was used for destruction, Nobel left his fortune (31 million SEK, equivalent to approximately $265 million today) to fund annual prizes for those who "have conferred the greatest benefit to humankind."

## Categories

Five original prizes (awarded since 1901):

- Physics: awarded by the Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences
- Chemistry: awarded by the Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences
- Physiology or Medicine: awarded by the Karolinska Institute in Stockholm
- Literature: awarded by the Swedish Academy
- Peace: awarded by the Norwegian Nobel Committee (Norway was in union with Sweden when Nobel wrote his will)

The Nobel Memorial Prize in Economic Sciences was added in 1968, funded by Sweden's central bank.

## The Ceremony

- Prizes announced in October each year
- Award ceremony held on December 10 (anniversary of Nobel's death) in Stockholm
- Peace Prize ceremony held in Oslo, Norway
- Laureates receive a gold medal, diploma, and monetary prize (11 million SEK per full prize in 2023)
- Nobel Banquet: formal dinner for 1,300 guests at Stockholm City Hall

## Notable Laureates

- Marie Curie: Physics (1903) and Chemistry (1911) — only person to win in two different sciences
- Albert Einstein: Physics (1921) for the photoelectric effect
- Martin Luther King Jr.: Peace (1964) for nonviolent civil rights activism
- Watson, Crick, and Wilkins: Physiology or Medicine (1962) for DNA structure
- Malala Yousafzai: Peace (2014) — youngest laureate at age 17

## Statistics

- Total prizes awarded (1901-2023): 621 prizes to 1,000 individuals and organizations
- Most prizes by country: United States (~400), United Kingdom (~130), Germany (~110)
- Women laureates: 65 (6.5% of total) — increasing in recent decades
- Average age of laureates: approximately 60 years
- Declined prizes: Jean-Paul Sartre (Literature, 1964), Le Duc Tho (Peace, 1973)

## Controversies

- Exclusion of mathematics (Fields Medal fills this gap)
- Limited to three recipients per prize (excludes many contributors)
- Cannot be awarded posthumously (since 1974)
- Some Peace Prize choices have been controversial
- Lack of diversity among laureates (improving slowly)
